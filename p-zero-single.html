Monte Carlo Simulation

Runs your current plan many times, each with a different random sequence of yearly returns and inflation, to reveal sequence-of-returns risk that a single fixed rate hides. Everything else in your plan stays exactly as configured — only returns and inflation vary.

Historical resamples real 1972–2025 return+inflation pairs in 5-year blocks — preserving actual crash-and-recovery sequences and the real return/inflation relationship of each year. This is a true stress-test against history's actual pattern of good and bad timing. Parametric instead draws returns from a bell curve around your own ROI input. Ending-balance figures are shown in today's dollars, but note a multi-decade horizon at historical average returns genuinely compounds to large numbers — focus on the success rate and the shape of the fan, not the absolute peak.

Adds the 1929 Great Depression and the 1966–1982 stagflation era to the sampling pool — the worst sequences a retiree could face, which the modern 1972–2025 window leaves out. Turning this on makes the test more pessimistic (and more honest about tail risk); expect the success rate to drop. Off by default so the baseline reflects the modern market regime. (Historical method only.)

How to read this — and how to think about Monte Carlo

The success rate is your bottom line. It's the share of simulated futures where the money lasted to Target EOL. It's also the most stable number here — it barely moves between runs. If you take one thing away, take this.

The percentiles are thresholds, not floors. "10th pct = $X" means the worst 10% of runs ended at or below $X — it does not mean $X is the worst case. Runs below it may have ended near zero or failed entirely years early (a plan that went bankrupt in 2050 counts as ~$0 ending wealth but really failed far worse). So the true downside lives below the 10th percentile, which is exactly why the success rate — not the 10th percentile — is the number to trust for "will I be OK."

Tail numbers wobble between runs; that's sampling noise. Each run draws a fresh random set of sequences, so the 10th and 90th percentiles jump around (you might see $1,400 one run and $700K the next). The median moves less; the success rate least of all. To steady the percentiles, raise the simulation count — 1,000 is decent, 5,000 is stable.

What Monte Carlo is for. The main projection assumes the same return every year, so it can't see sequence-of-returns risk — the danger that a crash lands in your early retirement years, when withdrawals from a depressed portfolio do permanent damage. The same average return in a different order can mean comfort or ruin. Monte Carlo replays hundreds of realistic orderings to reveal that spread. It answers "how robust is my plan to bad timing," not "what will happen."

1. Overview & Purpose

P-ZERO is a deterministic, year-by-year retirement simulation engine for a single filer planning a phased early retirement. Starting from 2026, it projects the growth, drawdown, tax liability, and longevity of five asset pools — Traditional (pre-tax), Roth, Taxable Brokerage, HSA, and 529 — plus any number of illiquid Real Estate / Business (passive LP) positions with their own distributions and capital events (Section 15b) — across a multi-decade horizon, through three life phases: full-time accumulation, semi-retirement (part-time 1099 or W-2 income), and full retirement.

"Deterministic" means one fixed path: every year uses the same nominal return (default 6%) and the same inflation rate (default 3%). This makes each year's math fully traceable — you can pick any row and reconcile every dollar — which is the tool's core strength. The deterministic core does not model market volatility; a good year and a crash year look identical. To test that risk directly, the Monte Carlo tool (top nav) re-runs the whole plan hundreds of times over random return sequences — see Section 18.

The engine runs each year through a consistent pipeline: determine life phase from your age → apply contributions (accumulation only) → compute forced RMDs → compute Roth conversions (bracket-filling) → pay tuition from 529 → pay healthcare from HSA → iteratively solve the tax-and-withdrawal loop (Section 8) → apply investment growth per your chosen timing convention (Section 17) → record the row.

Beyond the projection, four tools (top nav) help you act on it: Compare pins two plans side by side; Solve For inverts the model to find max spend, earliest retirement, or required portfolio; Monte Carlo stress-tests against random return sequences; and the seven KPI cards surface horizon, end wealth, after-tax legacy to heirs, lifetime tax, ACA subsidy, and 529 status. All are documented below.

The simulation runs entirely in your browser. No data is sent to any server; all calculations happen locally in JavaScript.

Important — for illustration and educational purposes only. P-ZERO is a personal planning sandbox. It is not financial, tax, investment, or legal advice, and nothing here is a recommendation to take (or not take) any action. Every projection rests on simplified assumptions — a single fixed return, approximated tax tables, hand-entered historical data, and many modeling shortcuts documented in Sections 19 and 20 — so real-world outcomes will differ, potentially by a lot. Do not make financial decisions based on this tool. Consult a qualified financial advisor, CPA, or attorney about your individual circumstances before acting.

2. Dollar Display Modes — and Why Ending ≠ Next Year's Starting

The engine always runs its internal math in nominal (future) dollars — this is required for correct tax-bracket, RMD, and Social Security calculations, since those are all defined in nominal-dollar terms. The Today's $ / Future $ toggle only changes how results are displayed:

  • Future $ (nominal): the raw dollar amounts that will actually move through the accounts in that calendar year.
  • Today's $ (inflation-adjusted, default): every figure in a given row is divided by that row's cumulative inflation factor (1.03n for year n at 3% inflation), converting it to today's purchasing power.

Important: in Today's $ mode, a year's Ending balance will NOT equal the next year's Starting balance. This is correct behavior, not a bug.

Internally the balances flow with perfect continuity: the nominal Ending balance of year N is exactly the nominal Starting balance of year N+1 (you can verify this by switching to Future $ mode, where the numbers match to the penny). But in Today's $ mode, year N's row is deflated by year N's inflation factor, while year N+1's row is deflated by year N+1's (larger) factor. The same nominal balance is therefore shown about 3% smaller in the next row — because a year of inflation has genuinely eroded its purchasing power.

Example — Say a year ends with a nominal spendable balance of $3,000,000. The next year's Starting balance is the same money, so in Future $ mode it still shows $3,000,000. In Today's $ mode that identical figure instead shows $3,000,000 ÷ 1.03 ≈ $2,912,621 (at 3% inflation). Nothing was lost — that money simply buys 3% less than a year earlier. Toggle Dollars on any row to watch the same balance convert by exactly one year of inflation.

This is an unavoidable property of real-dollar display: you can have within-row reconciliation (Starting + Contribs + Growth − outflows = Ending, all at one year's price level) or across-row continuity, but not both. This tool preserves within-row reconciliation, which is what makes each year's math auditable.

Sidebar inputs (balances, contributions, spending targets, healthcare costs, gift amounts, tuition costs) are always entered in today's dollars and are inflated internally to the nominal amount for the year they apply. The one exception is starting balances, which are 2026 dollars by definition (today = 2026 in this model).

Not to be confused with the KPI Numbers toggle sitting next to this one. Dollars changes what the numbers mean (today's purchasing power vs. nominal future dollars) and affects every figure in the app. KPI Numbers: Compact / Exact only changes how the KPI cards are written ($1.31M vs $1,309,908) — it touches no math and never affects the projection table. See section 4 for that one.

3. Input Parameters Explained

Household Core

  • Birth month/year: your birth month and year. Age is computed per calendar year (age = year − birth year) and drives every age-based trigger: retirement phases, catch-up eligibility, penalty thresholds, Medicare at 65, the RMD age, and Social Security claiming.
  • Target EOL (default 95): The simulation runs until you exceed this age. It also defines the "Portfolio Horizon" success test and the year used for the Net Worth at EOL KPI.

Age Milestones

  • Semi-Retirement Age (default 55): Drawdown begins when you step down from full-time work. Contributions stop, semi-retirement income begins (default $25K, running from your semi-retirement age to your full-retirement age), healthcare costs begin, and portfolio withdrawals start covering any shortfall.
  • Full Retirement Age (default 60): Full retirement begins when you reach your full-retirement age.
  • RMD age (default 75): When you reach this age, forced minimum distributions from the Traditional bucket begin (Section 11).

Annual Contributions (accumulation years only)

  • Defaults (2026): Traditional $24,500 • Roth $47,500 (via mega-backdoor) • Brokerage $36,000 • HSA $4,400 (single-coverage max) • 529 $0 — all entered in today's dollars and inflated each year. The Traditional + Roth defaults fill the $72,000 total 401(k) addition limit; adjust any of them to match your actual savings plan. The Fill Max Contributions button sets Traditional, Roth, and HSA to that year's base IRS limits in one click (Roth becomes $54,500 = $47,500 mega-backdoor headroom + $7,000 backdoor Roth IRA; the on-load default omits the IRA for conservatism). It fills base limits only — the Catch-Up toggle adds the 50+/55+ amounts on top automatically, so nothing is double-counted. Brokerage and 529 are left untouched since they're personal choices, not IRS-capped.
  • Catch-up toggle: when on, adds the age-based IRS catch-ups (inflation-scaled): $8,000/yr to Traditional+Roth once you are 50+, and $1,000/yr to HSA once you are 55+. It also applies the SECURE 2.0 “super catch-up” — for ages 60–63 the 401(k) catch-up rises to $11,250 (this replaces, not adds to, the $8,000), reverting to $8,000 at 64. The engine splits the 401(k) catch-up evenly between Traditional and Roth, and applies each amount only in years you are still saving (contributions stop at semi-retirement), so an early retirement can shorten or skip these windows.
  • Live catch-up hints: when the Catch-Up toggle is on, a small green line appears under Traditional, Roth, and HSA showing the catch-up-inclusive amount and the age window it applies to (e.g. “→ $28,500 (50–59)”). When your saving window reaches the 60–63 super-catch-up band, a second line shows that higher tier. These are display-only — they never change the values in the input boxes, because the catch-up applies conditionally by year inside the projection, not as a flat addition to your base contribution.
  • Brokerage contributions increase the brokerage cost basis dollar-for-dollar.
  • Real Estate / Business Investments — an optional, repeatable list of passive LP / syndication positions, each with its own capital, value, appreciation, distribution (and taxable %), optional refinance, and exit year/value plus recapture %. Empty by default, so it changes nothing until you add a deal. Full detail in Section 15b.

Asset Pool Balances & Brokerage Basis

  • Defaults: Traditional $500,000 • Roth $250,000 • Brokerage $500,000 • HSA $100,000 • 529 $100,000 (starting values).
  • Brokerage Basis % (default 60%): the share of the brokerage balance that is original after-tax principal. Only the remaining gain portion of each brokerage withdrawal is taxed (at LTCG rates). The basis ratio is tracked and updated every year as contributions add basis and withdrawals consume it proportionally.
  • Brokerage Dividend Yield (default 2%) & % Qualified (default 85%): the annual dividend the taxable account throws off, and what share of it is "qualified" — the long-held-stock kind taxed at the lower long-term capital-gains rates, versus ordinary dividends (REITs, bond/money-market funds) taxed at regular income rates. See Section 12.

Distribution Strategy

  • Annual Living Target — Excl. Healthcare (default $90,000, today's $): the net, after-tax amount you want available for non-healthcare living expenses each retirement year. Taxes do NOT come out of this number — the engine grosses up the portfolio withdrawal so that taxes are paid on top and the full target lands in your pocket (Section 8). Healthcare, gifting, and tuition are also tracked separately on top.
  • Distribution model: fixed dollar target (above) or a percentage of the portfolio.
  • Withdrawal Order: Sequential (Brokerage → Traditional → Roth) or Pro-Rata. See Section 9.
  • Healthcare (Pre-65) $20,000 / Post-65 Medicare $9,000: all-in annual costs, today's $ — premiums plus deductibles, copays, prescriptions, dental and vision, not premiums alone. These escalate at the Healthcare Inflation rate (4.5% default), not general CPI. See Section 14 for the split-age transition, the ACA subsidy inputs (toggle, $14K benchmark premium, household size, preserve-subsidy cap) and the IRMAA toggle.
  • Growth Timing: when contributions/withdrawals earn growth within a year. Default "Realistically Conservative" (half-year convention). See Section 17.

Roth Conversion, Social Security, Children

  • Roth conversion controls (toggle — off by default; start — "Auto" (shown as your age and start year, e.g. "56 (2036)"), or any manual age — bracket-fill cap or fixed amount) — Section 10.
  • Social Security controls (toggle — off by default, claim age and amount, COLA, taxable %) — Section 13.
  • Children profiles (birth years, undergrad/grad costs and durations) and the gifting schedule — Section 15.
  • Filing status is Single, switching to Head of Household automatically when you have dependents — documented in Section 7.

Macro Inputs & Tax Tables

  • Nominal ROI (default 6%): applied identically to all five buckets every year.
  • Inflation (default 3%): inflates spending targets, healthcare, contributions, tax brackets, the standard deduction, and gift/tuition amounts; also drives the Today's $ deflator.
  • Federal ordinary brackets, LTCG brackets, standard deduction ($15,750 Single / $23,625 Head of Household), the IRS Uniform Lifetime RMD divisors, and the IRMAA tiers are read-only reference values shown in the Federal Tax Details tab — sourced from the app's built-in tax-year data (IRS_2026), not editable, and never saved in a profile. Bracket thresholds are inflation-indexed each simulated year. Your personal settings — state tax (2.90%), county/local tax (1.5%), the “State Exempts Social Security” choice, and the heir tax rates for the After-Tax Legacy KPI — are editable in the left input panel (State & Local Tax and Legacy / Heir Assumptions sections) and are saved with your profile.

4. KPI Cards Explained

KPI number format — Compact vs Exact

KPI cards show compact figures by default — $1.31M, $694K — using the same convention as the net-worth chart axis. Millions carry two decimals on purpose: at this scale a $10,000 difference between two scenarios is still visible ($1.31M vs $1.32M), which matters when you are comparing plans.

  • Hover any card to swap that card's figures to exact dollars ($1,309,908) in place. The type shrinks slightly so nothing reflows.
  • KPI Numbers: Compact / Exact (beside the Dollars toggle) pins every card to exact figures permanently — useful for screenshots and on touch devices, where hover does not exist.

This is display-only: it changes no math, and the projection table always shows full exact dollars regardless of this setting. The table is the place to go for precision; the cards are for scanning. Compact figures are also what keeps all cards on a single row — the row sizes itself to however many cards are visible (5 by default, up to 7 if you hold RE/Business deals and the plan earns an ACA subsidy).

4b. Distribution Mix (where each year's money moves)

The chart area at the top has two tabs. Net Worth Trajectory shows balances growing and drawing down over the whole plan; Distribution Mix answers a different question for a single year — where did the money come from, and what did it pay for? It is a cash-flow (Sankey) view, not a balance view.

Three panels default to life-phase milestones (semi-retirement, full retirement, and Social Security claim age). Each panel tracks its milestone automatically — change a birth year or a semi/full-retirement age and the panels follow to the new years. Pick a specific year from a panel’s dropdown to pin it there instead; a pinned panel keeps your year even as other inputs change, and a ↺ reset button appears to hand it back to automatic tracking. Each panel shows Sources on the left flowing into Spend on the right, with a running total for each side.

  • Sources: Traditional, Roth, and Brokerage withdrawals; Social Security; RE/Business distributions; 1099 / part-time income (gross); HSA; and the 529 (the portion of tuition it funds).
  • Spend: Living, Healthcare, Taxes (the year's full tax, including any capital-gains tax), Gifting, and Tuition.
  • Ribbon thickness is proportional to each flow's share, with a small minimum so tiny slices stay visible. Labels show the rounded figure and share; hover any ribbon for the exact dollar amount and precise percent.
  • The numbers reconcile to the projection table for the same year, and each panel's Sources and Spend totals balance to the dollar. The view honors the Today's $ / Future $ toggle. Each bucket (Traditional, Roth, Brokerage, HSA & 529) has a hidden-by-default detail group — reveal them with the "Show Detail" buttons above the table. Columns follow a consistent pattern: Starting / Contribs / Growth / Dists / Ending, plus bucket-specific columns:

  • 529: Dists are tuition payments per the Children schedule (Section 15). Not part of the Combined columns.

7. Life Phase Logic

The model moves you through several phases, driven entirely by when you reach your semi-retirement age (the “Semi-Retirement Age” input) and full-retirement age (the “Full Retirement Age” input). Phases aren't tied to calendar dates — they're derived from your age, so they shift automatically with whatever ages you enter. Real Estate / Business distributions are phase-independent — they flow in every year a deal is held, in accumulation as much as in full retirement, and their capital events (refinance, exit) fire on the years you set regardless of phase. See Section 15b.

Phase 1 — Accumulation

Working full-time, from the start of the projection until you reach your semi-retirement age. All five contribution streams flow (inflation-indexed, plus catch-ups when eligible). No withdrawals, no healthcare cost modeled (employer coverage assumed), no semi-retirement income.

Example — Our filer (born 1980) accumulates from 2026 until reaching semi-retirement age 55 in 2035.

Phase 1.5 — Covered (not applicable to a single filer)

The “Covered” bridge phase only exists for a two-earner household — one spouse retired while the other still works full-time. A single filer has no spouse, so this phase never occurs: you move directly from full-time work (Phase 1) into semi-retirement (Phase 2) at your semi-retirement age.

Phase 2 — Semi-Retirement

Begins the year you leave full-time work, at your semi-retirement age. Contributions stop entirely. Semi-retirement income begins — 1099 self-employment (15.3% SE tax, with the ½-SE income-tax deduction) or W-2 employee income (7.65% FICA, no ½ deduction) by your choice — running from your semi-retirement age to your full-retirement age. Healthcare costs begin (pre-65 private rate). The living target, healthcare not covered by HSA, gifts, and all taxes are funded first by income, then by portfolio withdrawals. Roth conversions start per the Auto rule — the year after you fully step down — sized to the bracket room left after 1099 income and any Traditional spending draws; a manual start age can override Auto, but conversions are suppressed while you still work full-time.

Example — Semi-retirement starts in 2035 (age 55) with $25,000 of semi-retirement income, running until full-retirement age 60. Conversions begin in 2036.

Phase 3 — Full Retirement

Begins at your full-retirement age. By this point your semi-retirement income has ended, so its payroll tax (SE tax or FICA) is gone. The portfolio (plus Social Security, if enabled) funds everything. Medicare transition occurs at 65; RMDs begin at the RMD age (default 75); conversions continue while bracket room and Traditional balance remain.

Example — Full retirement begins in 2040, the year our filer reaches full-retirement age 60.

Filing Status — Single / Head of Household

This build files as a single taxpayer. If you add one or more dependents (in the Children section), filing status switches automatically to Head of Household, which applies the wider HoH brackets and larger standard deduction. Both use the true IRS tables for that status — not a scaled approximation. There is no survivor/widow(er) scenario in the single-filer version (it applies only to married couples); if your situation is married-filing-jointly, use the MFJ version instead.

8. The Iterative Tax & Withdrawal Solver

Determining the year's withdrawal creates a circular problem: the withdrawal must cover taxes, but the taxes depend on which accounts the withdrawal comes from — Traditional draws add ordinary income (potentially crossing brackets), brokerage draws create capital gains, and pre-59½ draws add penalties. More withdrawal → more tax → more withdrawal.

The engine resolves this with an iterative solve, run fresh for every simulated year:

  1. Compute the base need: Planned Dist + healthcare not covered by HSA + gifts, minus cash sources (gross semi-retirement income + Social Security). This seeds the withdrawal estimate with no taxes yet.
  2. Simulate the account draws for that withdrawal per the chosen sequence, capped at available balances.
  3. Size the bracket-fill Roth conversion for this iteration (spending-aware): fill only the room remaining between (existing ordinary income + those elective Traditional spending draws) and the chosen bracket cap, further capped at the Traditional balance left after spending draws. Fixed-amount conversions are sized up-front instead and skip this step.
  4. Compute the full tax stack for that draw mix: federal ordinary tax on (income − ½SE deduction + RMD + elective Traditional draws + Roth conversion + taxable SS − standard deduction), LTCG on the brokerage gain portion (stacked on top of ordinary income against the LTCG brackets), state + county, SE tax, and the 10% early-withdrawal penalty on Trad/Roth draws if you are under 59½.
  5. Compute the ACA subsidy from this iteration's MAGI (when enabled), net it against the marketplace premium, apply the HSA to the remaining healthcare, and fold the uncovered healthcare into the need — the subsidy is discontinuous at the 400% FPL cliff, so an oscillation guard settles on the conservative no-subsidy branch if the solve ping-pongs across it.
  6. Set the new withdrawal = fixed needs + uncovered healthcare + that tax bill − cash sources; repeat from step 2 until the withdrawal changes by less than $1 (typically 3–6 iterations, hard cap 14).

Consequence — the living target is truly net: your $90K is what you keep. Taxes are grossed up on top of it and drawn additionally from the portfolio. In heavy Roth-conversion or Traditional-heavy years the total drain can be substantially larger than the target itself.

If the portfolio cannot supply the converged withdrawal (all accounts exhausted), the unmet portion is recorded as a funding shortfall — this is what triggers the Portfolio Horizon failure state (Section 4), even though small residual balances may technically remain.

9. Withdrawal Sequencing

Sequential (default): Brokerage → Traditional → Roth. Spend the taxable account first (only the gain portion is taxed, at favorable LTCG rates, and draining it early leaves low-taxable-income years for cheap Roth conversions), tap Traditional second (ordinary income, but often at low brackets by then), preserve Roth for last (tax-free growth compounds longest, best asset to inherit).

Pro-Rata: each year's withdrawal is split across Traditional, Roth, and Brokerage in proportion to their current balances. Smooths the tax character of withdrawals over time rather than concentrating account types in phases.

The solver (Section 8) prices both strategies correctly — Traditional portions generate ordinary tax, brokerage portions generate LTCG on the gain share, Roth portions are tax-free — and the resulting tax difference feeds back into the withdrawal size. Real Estate / Business positions sit outside this sequence entirely: they're illiquid, so they are never drawn on to cover a shortfall. Their distributions (and any refinance or sale proceeds) instead arrive as cash that reduces the withdrawal the sequence has to produce, and sale proceeds land in Brokerage where they become part of the liquid pool from that year on. See Section 15b. The HSA sits outside this sequence entirely (healthcare only), as does the 529 (tuition only).

10. Roth Conversion Ladder

The conversion ladder is off by default (the RMD tax-torpedo and its IRMAA knock-on are a genuine argument for conversions, but whether they pay off is scenario-specific — so the conservative baseline leaves them off and lets you switch them on to test). When enabled (with a start age), each year the engine can move money from Traditional to Roth, paying ordinary income tax now in exchange for tax-free growth forever after and a smaller future RMD base. Two modes:

  • Fill bracket to X% (default) — spending-aware: the conversion is sized inside the iterative tax solver, after that year’s elective Traditional spending draws are known. It fills only the room remaining between (existing ordinary income + Traditional spending draws) and the top of your chosen bracket, so combined ordinary income lands at the cap and never over it. Real Estate / Business distributions count here: their taxable portion is part of "existing ordinary income," so it consumes bracket room before the conversion is sized — a deal throwing off taxable distributions leaves less space to convert at your target bracket. A RE/BI exit gain is a capital gain, so it does not consume ordinary bracket room, but it does raise MAGI (Section 14) and can therefore tighten the ACA-subsidy cap. See Section 15b. Spending has priority over conversion for both bracket room and Traditional balance — in years where spending draws alone exceed the cap, the conversion is $0, exactly as a human advisor would size it. When "Cap Roth conversions to preserve subsidy" is on (the default) and a marketplace subsidy is live, the conversion is additionally capped so MAGI lands at the 400% FPL cliff rather than over it — unless spending alone already blew the cliff, in which case the subsidy is lost anyway and the bracket cap governs alone. Capped at the remaining Traditional balance; brackets are the actual federal, inflation-indexed table (shown read-only in the Federal Tax Details tab).
  • Fixed amount: converts a set dollar amount per year regardless of bracket position.

Start timing: the default is Auto — the year after you fully step down, avoiding bracket-filling on top of any unmodeled partial-year income in the transition year; any manual age from the same dropdown overrides it. Either way, conversions are suppressed while you still work full-time, since your income would invisibly occupy the same brackets. How the size responds to income: in a year where semi-retirement income already fills part of the lower brackets, the engine converts only the room left up to your chosen bracket cap; once your consulting income ends at full retirement and income drops, the remaining room widens and the conversion grows to fill it. The conversion appears in Roth Conv and Total Taxable, its tax lands in Taxes (and is grossed into the withdrawal), and the money itself moves Traditional → Roth without ever appearing in Portfolio W/D.

Because the conversion and the withdrawal/tax solve are computed together, the conversion’s tax is grossed into the year’s withdrawal and its LTCG-stacking effect on brokerage gains is fully priced. Fixed-amount mode remains deliberately naive (converts the set amount regardless of bracket position or spending draws). The 5-year seasoning rule on converted funds is not enforced (see Limitations).

11. Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)

Starting the year you reach the RMD age (default 75), the engine forces a distribution of Traditional balance ÷ the IRS Uniform Lifetime divisor for that age (shown read-only in the Federal Tax Details tab). RMDs are always fully taxable ordinary income and are included in Total Taxable.

RMD cash is used to fund the year's spending need first; any excess beyond what spending required is reinvested into the Brokerage account (adding to cost basis) — it never disappears. Simplification: the model holds one shared Traditional bucket keyed to your age.

12. Tax Calculations

Ordinary Income Tax

Applied to: semi-retirement income (1099 income is reduced by the ½ SE-tax deduction; W-2 wages are fully taxable), RMDs, elective Traditional withdrawals, Roth conversions, the taxable share of Social Security, and the taxable portion of Real Estate / Business distributions (Section 15b — you set what percentage of each deal's distribution is currently taxable; the sheltered remainder is untaxed). The standard deduction ($15,750 Single / $23,625 Head of Household, inflation-indexed) is subtracted, then the single-filer marginal brackets (thresholds inflation-indexed) apply. If you add one or more dependents, filing status switches automatically to Head of Household, which uses its own wider brackets and larger standard deduction.

Long-Term Capital Gains

Only the gain portion of brokerage withdrawals (per the tracked basis ratio) is LTCG income — plus the gain on a Real Estate / Business exit (Section 15b), which flows through this same machinery. It stacks on top of ordinary taxable income against the LTCG brackets (0%/15%/20% structure), so low-ordinary-income years can realize gains at 0%. State + county rates also apply to the gain.

Depreciation recapture (RE/BI exits only): the recapture slice of a sale — your Recapture % of invested capital, capped at the gain — is taxed at the 25% recapture rate rather than LTCG, and is added on top of the year's other taxes. See Section 15b.

Self-Employment Tax

15.3% on 92.35% of gross 1099 income during semi-retirement. The Social Security wage-base cap is deliberately not modeled. In law the 12.4% Social Security portion stops at the wage base ($184,500 for 2026) while the 2.9% Medicare portion continues uncapped, and an extra 0.9% Medicare surtax applies above $200,000 (single filer). This engine applies a flat 15.3% with no ceiling, so it is exact up to about $199,783 of gross 1099 income and overstates SE tax above that (by roughly $5,750/yr at $250,000 and $32,473/yr at $500,000). Semi-retirement consulting income is assumed to sit below the cap; if you model 1099 income above ~$200,000, treat the tax figure as conservative. Half of the SE tax — not half the income — is deducted from ordinary taxable income. Income is displayed gross and SE tax sits in the Taxes column, so it is counted exactly once in the cash-flow math.

State & Local Tax

A flat state rate (default 2.90%) plus a county/local rate (default 1.5%), both in the State & Local Tax section of the left input panel — set them to your own jurisdiction's rates. As modeled, both apply to ordinary income and to realized capital gains (i.e. the defaults assume a state that taxes long-term gains as ordinary income with no preferential rate, and fully taxes IRA/401(k) withdrawals and Roth conversions — adjust if your state differs). The “State Exempts Social Security” checkbox (default on) removes the taxable-SS portion from the state/local base, reflecting the many states that don't tax Social Security. State-specific personal exemptions, credits, and pension deductions are not modeled; verify your state and local rates and rules against your official state tax authority.

Early Withdrawal Penalty

10% federal penalty on elective Traditional and Roth draws while you are under 59½. Simplifications: keyed to your age; applied to full Roth draws (real Roth contribution basis can come out penalty-free); no 72(t)/SEPP, Rule of 55, or other exceptions.

Brokerage Dividend Drag

A taxable brokerage account pays out dividends each year — cash you owe tax on whether or not you sell anything, unlike the tax-sheltered Traditional/Roth/HSA buckets. That unavoidable annual tax is the "drag." The engine estimates it as Dividend Yield × brokerage balance (default 2%), split into a qualified slice and an ordinary slice. Qualified dividends (default 85% — the kind from U.S. and many foreign stocks held long enough) are taxed at the preferential long-term capital-gains rates (0/15/20%), stacked on top of ordinary income just like realized gains; the ordinary slice (REITs, money-market and bond funds, or stocks not held long enough) is taxed at regular income rates. A portfolio of broad U.S. index funds runs high (~85–100% qualified); heavy REIT/bond holdings pull it lower. Both are added to MAGI, so dividends can nudge you toward the ACA cliff or an IRMAA tier. The dividends themselves stay invested (they're part of the 6% return, not an extra inflow); because they are after-tax principal, reinvesting them adds to cost basis — which lowers the taxable gain on future sales. Net effect: a small tax paid every year, partly offset later by reduced capital-gains tax when those shares are sold. Set the yield to 0 to switch the whole feature off.

Not modeled

AMT, NIIT (3.8%), IRMAA surcharges, ACA premium subsidies/cliffs, state-specific 529 recapture, qualified dividends on un-sold holdings, tax-loss harvesting, and filing-status changes (e.g., survivor filing single).

13. Social Security

Off by default — the default projection deliberately excludes SS as a conservatism margin; toggle it on to include it. You set your claim age (default 70) and an annual benefit stated in today's dollars ($25,000 — intentionally a conservative haircut vs. statement estimates).

Benefits are indexed by the separate SS COLA input (default 3%) from 2026 — a simplification of how SSA actually indexes to claim age and applies COLA after. The editable "taxable %" (default 85%) of benefits joins ordinary taxable income; the true IRS provisional-income phase-in formula is not modeled (at this household's income level, 85% is almost always the real answer anyway). Benefits reduce the required portfolio withdrawal dollar-for-dollar in the solver. When the “State Exempts SS” checkbox is on, state/county tax is not applied to benefits. Spousal and survivor benefits are not modeled in this single-filer build.

14. Healthcare: MAGI, ACA Subsidies & IRMAA

The one-sentence mental model:

One yardstick — MAGI — is read by two programs: before 65, LOW MAGI earns you subsidies (ACA marketplace); after 65, HIGH MAGI earns you penalties (Medicare IRMAA). Everything below is detail on that sentence.

14.1 — The base costs (what you budget)

The two sidebar inputs are all-in gross household costs in today's dollars, escalated at the Healthcare Inflation rate (4.5% default) rather than general CPI: Pre-65 ($20,000 default) = unsubsidized marketplace premiums + deductibles + copays; Post-65 Medicare ($9,000 default) = Part B/D + Medigap + out-of-pocket. Costs begin at semi-retirement: the pre-65 rate applies until you reach 65, then the Medicare rate applies. Everything in this section only ever adjusts these bases: ACA can reduce the pre-65 side; IRMAA can increase the post-65 side; neither ever touches the other side of the 65 line.

Want the simple model? Turn both toggles off → the engine runs flat $20,000 / $9,000 (healthcare-inflation-indexed) with zero ACA/IRMAA adjustments — verified identical to the pre-ACA engine, cell for cell. The MAGI column and the Lifetime ACA Subsidy card hide automatically so the interface matches the simpler model.

14.2 — MAGI: the yardstick everything reads

MAGI (Modified Adjusted Gross Income) is the government's measure of "how much income did you really have this year," used for benefit eligibility. Crucially, no deductions reduce it — which is why it differs from the Total Taxable column. Real Estate / Business investments feed MAGI too (Section 15b): the taxable portion of distributions counts as ordinary income, and a sale/exit gain counts like a brokerage gain — so a big exit year can push you over the ACA cliff or into an IRMAA tier exactly as a large conversion would.

14.3 — ACA subsidies before 65: the math, worked

Within eligibility, you contribute a sliding 2.1%–9.96% of MAGI toward the benchmark premium (the SLCSP input — the subsidizable slice of the $20,000 pre-65 cost; the out-of-pocket remainder is never subsidized); the subsidy pays the rest. The big number in the cell is always the TOTAL you spend that year. The small sub-line is the explanation of how it got there — never an addition on top:

Combining an ACA reduction and an IRMAA surcharge in the same figure is impossible: ACA only touches the pre-65 base (downward), IRMAA only touches the post-65 base (upward), and they never apply to the same year since one is strictly before 65 and the other strictly after.

14.5 — IRMAA after 65: the penalty, worked

Medicare charges each enrolled person a premium surcharge when MAGI from two years earlier crossed a tier — so an age-63 decision sets an age-65 bill.

It's not a rolling window or a multi-year penalty. It's a one-to-one mapping with a 2-year delay. Each year's Medicare premium is determined by the MAGI from exactly one tax year — two years prior (the most recent return the SSA has on file). So:

  • One high-MAGI year → exactly one penalized year, arriving two years later. Not "penalized for the next 2 years."
  • Each year resets independently. There's no memory, no averaging, no persistence.
  • Sustained high MAGI does produce a sustained surcharge — but only because every high year generates its own penalized year downstream. The penalty stream is simply your MAGI stream, shifted two years and run through the tier table.
  • The danger zone therefore starts at age 63, not 65: age-63 MAGI sets the first Medicare premium. High MAGI at 62 or earlier maps to premium years before enrollment and never bites — the classic reason to finish big Roth conversions by 62.

14.7 — "The ACA toggle doesn't seem to do anything"

The toggle doesn't create subsidies — low MAGI does. The toggle only permits the model to count them. If every marketplace-eligible year has MAGI over the cliff (e.g., with conversions off, Traditional funds those years at ~$500K MAGI), the subsidy is $0 in every year and the toggle changes nothing — correctly. Diagnostic checklist: (1) Lifetime ACA Subsidy KPI reads $0? (2) Scan the MAGI column across pre-65 retirement years — is any year under ~$62,600? (3) If not, no toggle will help; only a strategy that changes the funding source of those years (conversions draining Traditional, Roth/basis-heavy withdrawals) can get you under the cliff.

Funding order (unchanged): the HSA pays healthcare first, tax-free, up to its balance (Medicare premiums and IRMAA are HSA-qualified); only the uncovered remainder enters the portfolio withdrawal and is grossed up for taxes. A subsidy therefore reduces real account outflows dollar-for-dollar — first preserving HSA, then shrinking Portfolio W/D. The living target still excludes healthcare entirely; these costs ride on top of the $149K. A 3-year subsidy extension has passed the House — the toggle exists precisely because this law may change.

15. Children: College Funding & Gifting

The Children section defines per-child education profiles that drive the 529's tuition schedule automatically — nothing is hardcoded. For each child: birth year, annual undergrad cost and years, and an optional grad-school block with its own cost and years. Undergrad starts the year the child turns 18; grad school starts immediately after undergrad ends. All costs are today's dollars, inflated to each tuition year.

Defaults: two children (born 2012 and 2015), each $30,500/yr × 4 undergrad years and $50,000/yr × 3 grad years — producing overlapping tuition spanning roughly a decade, including years where one child's grad school overlaps the other's undergrad. (Undergrad starts the year a child turns 18; grad follows immediately, so the exact calendar span shifts with each child's birth year.)

Funding: tuition is paid from the 529 first. If the 529 runs dry, the shortfall is paid from Brokerage: basis is reduced pro-rata and the appreciated portion of that sale is realized as a long-term capital gain, taxed with your other gains that year (stacked on ordinary income). The 529 Status KPI flips to “Underfunded.” The 529 burn for actual tuition is itself tax-free (a qualified education distribution); only the brokerage sale that covers a shortfall is taxed.

Gifting schedule: year/amount rows for one-off transfers (weddings, home down-payments, major gifts). Amounts are today's dollars — the engine inflates them to the gift year so the recipient gets the intended real value — and they appear in the Life Event column, funded through the normal withdrawal sequence.

15b. Real Estate / Business Investments

This section models passive private investments where you're a limited or non-managing partner — real-estate syndications (apartment complexes, storage, industrial), business LP stakes, and private funds. These behave very differently from the five liquid buckets: they throw off cash distributions while you hold them, return capital on a refinance, and pay a lump on exit/sale, and they are illiquid — you can't draw on them to cover a shortfall until a capital event converts them to cash. Add as many deals as you like (the list works like the Children/Gifting lists).

Per-deal inputs (the panel groups these into Position, Income and Capital Events):

  • Invested (Basis) — what you put in; your cost basis for the exit-gain calculation.
  • Current Value & Appreciation %/Yr — the position's value today and how fast it grows. (In Today's-$ mode the displayed value is deflated by inflation, so a position that only appreciates at ~inflation holds roughly flat in real terms.)
  • Annual Distribution & Distribution Taxable % — the cash yield and the fraction of it that's currently taxable. Syndication distributions are frequently shielded by depreciation (the K-1 often shows a paper loss), so only part is taxed as ordinary income; the default 25% approximates that shelter. Set it to 100% for a fully-taxable income stream, or lower for a heavily-sheltered one.
  • Dist. COLA — whether the distribution grows with inflation or stays flat in nominal dollars.
  • Refi Year / Refi Capital — an optional mid-hold refinance that returns a chunk of your capital as tax-free return of capital (it reduces your remaining basis) and lands in Brokerage. The year is a calendar year (2030, not "year 4"), chosen from the dropdown; pick — for no refinance.
  • Exit Year / Exit Value — when the deal sells and for how much. The year is a calendar year (2035, not "year 5"), chosen from the dropdown; pick — to hold the position indefinitely (through end of life). The deal is held through the year before the exit year, sells in the exit year, and shows nothing afterwards.
  • Recapture % — see the tax treatment below.
Reading the value column in Today's $ — the RE/BI value shown in Today's $ mode is the real value: nominal appreciation less inflation. With the default 4% appreciation against 3% inflation, the position climbs slowly (about 1%/yr in real terms). Set appreciation equal to your inflation rate and the column goes perfectly flat every year — that is correct, not a bug: growing 3% while prices rise 3% is zero real gain, so the position holds exactly its purchasing power and buys the same basket forever. Switch Dollars to Future $ to watch the nominal value compound ($100,000 → $104,000 → $108,160…) and the distributions grow with COLA.

How the money flows through the plan: distributions are added to your income for the year (reducing how much you must withdraw from the portfolio), and their taxable portion flows through the same ordinary-income machinery as everything else — so it counts toward your tax brackets, your state/local tax, your ACA/IRMAA MAGI, and the room a bracket-filling Roth conversion has before it hits your target bracket. Refinance and sale proceeds are deposited into Brokerage; whatever isn't needed for that year's spending and taxes is reinvested there as after-tax principal (so it isn't taxed again as gain later).

Exit taxation (accurate & deliberately conservative): when a deal sells, the proceeds are split three ways:

  • Return of capital (your remaining basis, net of any prior refinance) — not taxed.
  • Depreciation recapture — Recapture % of your invested capital (capped at the total gain) is taxed at the 25% recapture rate. This approximates the depreciation you benefited from during the hold coming due at sale. Set it to 0 to ignore recapture; a modest default (e.g. 15–20%) is realistic for a cost-segregated syndication. Because recapture is a real tax the simple view would omit, including it makes exits show more tax and lower net proceeds — the conservative, and more accurate, direction.
  • Remaining gain — taxed at long-term capital-gains rates, stacked on top of your ordinary income exactly like a brokerage gain (so it also raises MAGI for ACA/IRMAA and interacts with the conversion cap).

Net worth & legacy — the two-tier view: because these positions are illiquid, the app keeps them visually separate from your spendable money. The Net Worth at EOL KPI shows Liquid Net Worth as the primary number (the spendable portfolio you can actually draw on), with a smaller incl. RE/Biz line beneath it, and a dedicated Real Estate / Business (illiquid) KPI tile calls out the held value on its own. Both the total line and the tile appear only when you have at least one deal. At death, positions still held pass to heirs with a step-up in basis — the built-in gain is erased — so they flow into the After-Tax Legacy at 100%, the same favorable treatment as brokerage. To see the year-by-year activity (start value, distributions, refi, sale, end value), toggle the Real Estate / Business detail column-group above the projection table.

Deliberate simplifications: recapture is approximated as a percentage of invested capital rather than tracked from an actual depreciation schedule; there's no modeling of capital calls, deal-level debt/leverage beyond the net figures you enter, K-1 passive-activity-loss carryforwards, 1031 exchanges, or Opportunity-Zone rules. Enter your own net figures and the model handles the cash-flow and tax mechanics faithfully at the planning level. As always, this is a planning sandbox, not tax advice — confirm specifics with your CPA.

16. 529 & HSA: Spendable vs Total Net Worth

The app draws a deliberate line between spendable retirement wealth (Traditional + Roth + Brokerage + HSA) and total net worth (those four + 529):

  • The Combined table columns, the Portfolio Horizon KPI, and the Net Worth at EOL KPI all use spendable wealth — a leftover 529 can never mask a depleted retirement portfolio.
  • The 529 remains fully visible in its own detail section, and the net-worth chart has independent toggles (HSA shown by default; 529 hidden by default); axis labels use compact $X.XXM formatting while the hover tooltip shows exact dollars per bucket.
  • The HSA counts as spendable because healthcare is a genuine, guaranteed living expense that the HSA directly funds tax-free — but the model only ever uses it for healthcare, never general spending.

529 contribution timing: unlike your retirement contributions (which run until you semi-retire), the 529 contribution is tied to education, not to your retirement date. It flows only while you have children modeled and stops the year your youngest child starts college (age 18) — after that point the 529 is being drawn down to pay tuition, so continuing to fund it would over-contribute to an account whose non-education withdrawals are penalized. With multiple children, contributions continue until the last (youngest) one starts, keeping every child's education funded. If no children are modeled, the 529 contribution never flows, since a 529 has no purpose without a beneficiary.

If you ever raided the 529 for non-education spending (not modeled): contributions come out tax- and penalty-free; only the earnings portion is taxed as ordinary income plus a 10% federal penalty. A beneficiary can also roll up to $35,000 (lifetime) of a long-held 529 into their Roth IRA under current rules. State tax-credit recapture varies by state and isn't modeled.

Monte Carlo Simulation

Everything else in this tool is deterministic: every year earns exactly your ROI input, so "95+ Secure" really means "secure on the average path." Monte Carlo (top-nav button) instead runs your plan hundreds of times, each with a different random sequence of yearly returns and inflation, and reports how often it survives — exposing sequence-of-returns risk: a crash early in retirement is far more damaging than the same crash late, something a flat rate cannot see. It's the single most important lens for a long horizon that starts with big withdrawals. Only returns and inflation vary; every other input (spending, contributions, conversions, SS, ages, taxes) stays exactly as you set it, and your live plan is untouched — inputs are snapshotted and restored around the run.

Two methods, two questions:

  • Historical block bootstrap resamples real 1972–2025 return sequences in 5-year blocks — sampling each year's actual return and its matching inflation together, so both the crash-and-recovery momentum and the real return/inflation relationship stay intact. No recentering: it tests your plan against history's genuine pattern of good and bad timing. Because the sampled sequences carry history's actual average return (which was generous), nominal balances over a multi-decade horizon can compound to large numbers — the tool deflates ending values to today's dollars, but focus on the success rate and the fan's shape rather than the absolute peak. An optional stress mode extends the sampling pool back to 1928, adding the 1929 Great Depression and the 1966–1982 stagflation era — the worst sequences a retiree could face, which the modern window omits. It’s off by default (baseline = modern regime); turning it on is a deliberate tail-risk stress test and will lower the success rate.
  • Parametric normal draws each year independently from a bell curve centered on your ROI input with an editable volatility (default 12%, roughly a 70/30 portfolio). With no mean reversion and full volatility drag, it's deliberately more pessimistic. It answers "how robust is this around my assumption?"

Output (ephemeral — shown only after a run, closable): a success-rate headline (green ≥90%, amber ≥75%, red below), a fan chart of net worth over time (solid median with a shaded 10th–90th-percentile band), and the ending-net-worth percentiles. A rough rule of thumb is that 85–90%+ is a comfortable plan; lower means the plan works on average but is fragile to bad timing. Because MC honors your current toggles, running it with conversions on vs. off (or different spending) is a real robustness test, not just an average-case one.

Failure timing. When some runs fail, the results also report two depletion years: the typical failure (the median of all failed runs — the characteristic year a failing plan runs dry) and the earliest (the unluckiest run's depletion year). The earliest is the more volatile of the two — it shifts between runs and gets worse the more simulations you run, since more draws give bad luck more chances — so read it as "roughly how early could it go," not a fixed number.

Anatomy of a typical failure. If any runs failed, a small chart shows a representative failed run — the one whose depletion year sits at the median of all failures, so it reflects the characteristic failure rather than a freak extreme. It plots that run's year-by-year returns (losses in the first decade flagged in red) alongside its net-worth path sliding to depletion. Its purpose is to make sequence-of-returns risk visible: the failing runs almost always share the same signature — a market drop early in retirement, while withdrawals are largest relative to the balance, that sends the portfolio into a spiral it never recovers from. The specific run changes each time you press Run (it's a random draw), but the shape of the failure is the durable lesson.

How to read the results

  • Success rate is the bottom line — the share of runs where the money lasts to Target EOL, and the most stable figure (it barely moves between runs). Treat it as the "will I be OK?" number.
  • Percentiles are thresholds, not floors. "10th percentile = $X" means the worst 10% of runs ended at or below $X — not that $X is the worst case. Runs below it may have ended near zero or failed entirely years early (a plan bankrupt in 2050 shows ~$0 ending wealth but really failed far worse). The true downside lives below the 10th percentile, which is why the success rate is more trustworthy than any tail number.
  • The median is the middle, not the likely value. Half of runs end below it, half above; the outcomes are spread across the whole range, not clustered at the median. "50% chance of ending above the median" is correct; "high chance of ending near the median" is not.
  • Tail numbers wobble; that's sampling noise. Each run draws a fresh random set of sequences, so the 10th/90th percentiles jump around between runs. The median moves less, the success rate least. Raise the simulation count (1,000 decent, 5,000 stable) to steady the percentiles.

Methodology & data provenance

The historical method is a block bootstrap. The engine holds a hardcoded table of annual figures — S&P 500 total return, 10-year Treasury total return, and CPI inflation — for 1972–2025 (and 1928–1971 when stress mode is on). Each year those three are blended to a single portfolio return using a 70% stock / 30% bond mix, paired with that year's actual inflation. To build one simulated future, the engine picks a random 5-year block from history, then another, and another, until it has enough years to cover the horizon — sampling in blocks rather than single years so that real multi-year patterns (a crash followed by its recovery, a stagflation stretch) survive intact instead of being shuffled away. Returns and inflation always travel together as the same historical year's pair, preserving their real relationship. It runs the whole plan once per simulated future (hundreds of times), recording whether it survived and the year-by-year net worth, then reports the success rate and the 10/50/90 percentile bands.

The parametric method instead ignores history and draws each year's return independently from a normal (bell-curve) distribution centered on your ROI input, with an editable volatility. Because each year is independent, it has no mean reversion and suffers full volatility drag, making it deliberately more pessimistic than the historical method — a useful "what if the future is choppier than the past" counterweight.

Honest caveat on the data. The historical figures are hand-entered approximations of the public record, rounded and assembled for this tool — not a live feed from an authoritative source, and 2025 is partial/estimated. They are shape-accurate (the real crashes, recoveries, and inflation spikes are present in roughly the right magnitudes), which is what matters for stress-testing sequence risk, but they are not basis-point exact. Do not treat any specific percentile as precise. The data is frozen in the file and never updated at runtime; the app makes no network calls. This is a planning sandbox, not a market simulator — see the overall disclaimer.

Solve For (Max Spend / Earliest Retirement / Required Portfolio)

The Solve For button in the top nav inverts the model: instead of you setting an input and reading the outcome, it searches for the input value that hits a target outcome — running the full engine ~20 times behind the scenes (a binary search) to converge on the answer. Three targets:

  • Max sustainable annual spend — the highest Annual Living Target that still survives to Target EOL.
  • Earliest safe full-retirement year — the earliest year both spouses can fully retire with the plan still surviving; it sets both full-retirement ages to land on that year.
  • Required starting portfolio — scales your starting balances to the minimum that survives. You choose which buckets scale (all / spendable only / brokerage only), since "how much do I need" has different answers depending on which pot the money comes from.

Success means the portfolio never depletes before Target EOL and (if the cushion box is checked, default $250,000) ends at or above that cushion — uncheck it for the true bare-survival maximum. Crucially, every solve honors all your current toggles (SS, Roth conversions, ACA, IRMAA, survivor, dividends): "max spend" means max spend given everything else you've set, so flipping a toggle and re-solving shows how that lever moves the answer. The solved value is shown first; Apply to inputs writes it into the matching field (and offers Undo), or Cancel to leave your plan untouched. Probing never disturbs your inputs — they're snapshotted and restored around every search.

19. Key Assumptions & Simplifications

  • One fixed nominal return (6%) for every bucket, every year — no volatility, no glide path, no asset-allocation differences between account types. Real Estate / Business positions are the exception: each carries its own appreciation rate you set per deal (Section 15b), independent of the portfolio return.
  • One fixed inflation rate (3%) drives spending, healthcare, contributions, brackets, deductions, tuition, and gifts alike (SS has its own COLA input, defaulted equal).
  • Tax law is frozen: 2026 MFJ brackets/deduction inflate forever; no TCJA-style regime changes, no future legislation.
  • Single shared buckets per type (one Traditional, one Roth, etc.); RMDs and penalties key off your age.
  • Brokerage basis is tracked as a single blended ratio, not tax lots; each withdrawal realizes gains proportionally.
  • Contribution inputs are taken as-entered (inflation-indexed) — IRS contribution limits are not enforced.
  • Healthcare costs are flat within each tier (no age-based escalation beyond inflation, no long-term-care shock modeling).
  • All flows are annual; intra-year timing exists only through the growth convention.
  • The core projection is a single deterministic path (one fixed return every year); sequence-of-returns risk is tested separately via the optional Monte Carlo tool (top-nav button), which is not part of the deterministic engine.

20. Known Limitations

  • Early-withdrawal penalty. The 10% pre-59½ penalty applies to Traditional/Roth draws taken before the filer reaches 59½. Because a single filer owns every account, the age test is unambiguous here (unlike the MFJ version, which must pick one spouse's age for a pooled balance). Roth withdrawal ordering is still not modeled, so the penalty may be overstated on Roth draws — see below.
  • Roth 5-year seasoning rules (for conversions and earnings) are not enforced — early Roth access is modeled as available with only the 10% penalty pre-59½.
  • Roth withdrawal ordering (contributions → conversions → earnings) is not modeled; penalties may be overstated for Roth draws.
  • Social Security taxability is modeled as a flat, user-editable percentage (default 85%) rather than the statutory phase-in. In law the taxable share depends on provisional income (AGI excluding SS + tax-exempt interest + half the benefit): nothing is taxed below $32,000 (MFJ) / $25,000 (Single), up to 50% applies through $44,000 / $34,000, and up to 85% above that — thresholds frozen since 1993 and never inflation-indexed. The 85% default is exact for any plan whose provisional income clears the top threshold, which most retirees with meaningful portfolio income do permanently; it overstates the taxable share only for low-income retirees still inside the phase-in, so the simplification is conservative. Adjust the “% of SS Taxed” input if your plan sits in the phase-in band. Spousal/survivor benefits and claiming-age adjustments are likewise simplified to flat inputs.
  • ACA subsidies (pre-65) and IRMAA (post-65) are modeled as optional toggles (Section 14); when they are on, Roth-conversion sizing can additionally respect the ACA subsidy cliff. Both carry their own simplifications, listed below.
  • Fixed-amount Roth conversions are sized without regard to bracket position or same-year spending draws (by design); only bracket-fill mode is spending-aware.
  • Filing status is Single, or Head of Household when dependents are present, using the true IRS 2026 tables for each. Head-of-household eligibility is inferred simply from having one or more dependents; the qualifying-surviving-spouse status is not modeled (this is the single-filer build).
  • State/county taxes are flat rates with no deductions or credits; the only state-specific exclusion modeled is the optional Social Security exemption. Scheduled or conditional future rate changes are not modeled (the editable rate is fixed for the whole run).
  • Depletion detection uses a $100 nominal shortfall tolerance; sub-$100 annual gaps won't flag failure.
  • Real Estate / Business Investments (Section 15b) simplifications: depreciation recapture is approximated as a percentage of invested capital rather than tracked from a real depreciation schedule. Note that because these are passive LP / syndication stakes (not directly-owned property), depreciation's benefit is already modeled during the hold as the sheltered portion of distributions (the “distribution taxable %” input, default 25% — the K-1 typically shelters most of the cash). The invested figure you enter is therefore treated as your cost basis at exit and is not separately stepped down for depreciation — doing so would count the same depreciation twice (once as the hold-period shelter, again as extra gain at sale). Other simplifications: the position's value follows a single appreciation rate you set (no deal-level modeling of leverage, NOI, or cap-rate shifts); capital calls, K-1 passive-activity-loss carryforwards, 1031 exchanges, and Opportunity-Zone rules are not modeled; distributions are assumed to be received as entered (no modeling of missed or suspended distributions in troubled years). Enter your own net figures and the cash-flow and tax mechanics are handled faithfully at the planning level.
  • After-Tax Legacy simplifications: a single flat heir tax rate is applied to the whole inherited Traditional/HSA balance (real heirs drain it over up to 10 years across their own brackets); state estate/inheritance tax is not modeled (many states have none — verify yours), the federal estate exemption is assumed not binding, and no growth during the heirs' 10-year window is modeled.
  • Dividend simplification: dividends are modeled as a flat yield on the brokerage balance with a fixed qualified/ordinary split; real dividends vary by holding, and return-of-capital distributions and dividend growth over time are not separately modeled.
  • Covered-phase assumption: not applicable to a single filer — there is no second earner, so no Covered bridge phase ever occurs. (This assumption applies only to the married version, where one spouse keeps working while the other steps down.)
  • ACA simplifications: the applicable-percentage schedule is a fixed 2026 table (source-editable constant); MAGI below 138% FPL is floored rather than modeling Medicaid; premiums are not age-curved (one flat benchmark input); repayment/reconciliation of advance credits is not modeled; pending subsidy-extension legislation is handled only via the toggle.
  • IRMAA simplifications: tier thresholds are all inflation-indexed (in law the top tier is frozen until 2028); the lookback year's filing status is assumed to match the current year's; SSA-44 life-event appeals are not modeled.

21. Glossary

Nominal vs Real Dollars: Nominal = the literal dollar figures of a future year. Real (today's $) = nominal deflated by cumulative inflation to today's purchasing power. See Section 2.
Funding Shortfall: The portion of a year's required withdrawal the portfolio could not supply. The engine's true depletion signal.
Spendable Net Worth: Traditional + Roth + Brokerage + HSA — wealth available (directly or via healthcare) for retirement living. Excludes the 529.
Gross-Up: Withdrawing extra to cover the taxes generated by the withdrawal itself, so the target amount arrives net. Solved iteratively each year.
RMD: Required Minimum Distribution — IRS-mandated annual withdrawal from pre-tax accounts starting at the RMD age, computed from the Uniform Lifetime divisor table.
LTCG: Long-Term Capital Gains — the tax treatment of the gain portion of brokerage withdrawals; stacks on ordinary income against its own 0/15/20% brackets.
LP (Limited Partner): A passive, non-managing investor in a syndication, fund, or partnership. You contribute capital and receive distributions and a share of the sale proceeds, but don't operate the deal. Modeled in Section 15b.
Distribution (RE/BI): The periodic cash a private/LP position pays out while held. Only the taxable percentage you set is taxed as ordinary income — the rest reflects depreciation shelter (see Section 15b).
Depreciation Recapture: Tax due at sale on the depreciation benefit taken during the hold, at a 25% rate (higher than LTCG). Modeled as a percentage of invested capital, capped at the gain. Including it makes exits show more tax — the accurate and conservative direction.
Return of Capital: Cash back that represents your own principal rather than profit — a refinance payout, or the basis portion of sale proceeds. Not taxed; it reduces your remaining basis.
Step-Up in Basis: At death, an heir's cost basis in an inherited asset resets to its date-of-death value, erasing the built-in gain. Applies to Brokerage and to Real Estate / Business positions held at death — both pass into After-Tax Legacy at 100%.
Illiquid: Cannot be drawn on to fund spending. Real Estate / Business positions are illiquid until a refinance or exit converts them to cash — which is why the app shows Liquid Net Worth as the primary EOL number and the illiquid value separately.
Cost Basis: The after-tax principal in the brokerage account. Withdrawals return basis tax-free and realize gains proportionally.
SE Tax: Self-Employment tax (15.3% on 92.35% of 1099 income). Half of the tax itself is deductible from ordinary income.
Roth Conversion Ladder: Systematically converting Traditional to Roth in low-bracket years to reduce lifetime taxes and future RMDs.
Sequence Risk: The danger of poor returns early in retirement permanently impairing a portfolio under withdrawals. Not captured by this deterministic model.
Widow’s Penalty: The tax squeeze a surviving spouse faces when filing status shifts from MFJ to Single (narrower brackets, smaller deduction). Not modeled in this single-filer build — see the MFJ version.
Step-Up in Basis: At death, the decedent’s share of appreciated taxable assets resets its cost basis to market value, erasing that share’s unrealized gain for tax purposes.
MFJ: Married Filing Jointly — the assumed federal filing status throughout.
IRMAA: Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount — Medicare premium surcharges per enrolled person, based on MAGI from two years prior. Modeled (toggleable) with 2026 tiers.
MAGI: Modified Adjusted Gross Income — the benefit-eligibility yardstick (no deductions reduce it). Drives the ACA cliff pre-65 and IRMAA tiers post-65. See Section 14.2.
SLCSP / Benchmark Premium: The second-lowest-cost Silver marketplace plan — the reference premium the ACA subsidy is computed against.
Subsidy Cliff: Under post-2025 law, ACA premium credits end abruptly at 400% of FPL — one extra dollar of MAGI forfeits the entire subsidy.
ACA: Affordable Care Act marketplace coverage for pre-65 retirees. Subsidies (and their interaction with Roth-conversion MAGI) are modeled — see Section 14.
COLA: Cost of Living Adjustment applied to Social Security benefits (separate input from general inflation).
