Monte Carlo Simulation

Runs your current plan many times, each with a different random sequence of yearly returns and inflation, to reveal sequence-of-returns risk that a single fixed rate hides. Everything else in your plan stays exactly as configured — only returns and inflation vary.

Historical resamples real 1972–2025 return+inflation pairs in 5-year blocks — preserving actual crash-and-recovery sequences and the real return/inflation relationship of each year. This is a true stress-test against history's actual pattern of good and bad timing. Parametric instead draws returns from a bell curve around your own ROI input. Ending-balance figures are shown in today's dollars, but note a multi-decade horizon at historical average returns genuinely compounds to large numbers — focus on the success rate and the shape of the fan, not the absolute peak.

Adds the 1929 Great Depression and the 1966–1982 stagflation era to the sampling pool — the worst sequences a retiree could face, which the modern 1972–2025 window leaves out. Turning this on makes the test more pessimistic (and more honest about tail risk); expect the success rate to drop. Off by default so the baseline reflects the modern market regime. (Historical method only.)

How to read this — and how to think about Monte Carlo

The success rate is your bottom line. It's the share of simulated futures where the money lasted to Target EOL. It's also the most stable number here — it barely moves between runs. If you take one thing away, take this.

The percentiles are thresholds, not floors. "10th pct = $X" means the worst 10% of runs ended at or below $X — it does not mean $X is the worst case. Runs below it may have ended near zero or failed entirely years early (a plan that went bankrupt in 2050 counts as ~$0 ending wealth but really failed far worse). So the true downside lives below the 10th percentile, which is exactly why the success rate — not the 10th percentile — is the number to trust for "will I be OK."

Tail numbers wobble between runs; that's sampling noise. Each run draws a fresh random set of sequences, so the 10th and 90th percentiles jump around (you might see $1,400 one run and $700K the next). The median moves less; the success rate least of all. To steady the percentiles, raise the simulation count — 1,000 is decent, 5,000 is stable.

What Monte Carlo is for. The main projection assumes the same return every year, so it can't see sequence-of-returns risk — the danger that a crash lands in your early retirement years, when withdrawals from a depressed portfolio do permanent damage. The same average return in a different order can mean comfort or ruin. Monte Carlo replays hundreds of realistic orderings to reveal that spread. It answers "how robust is my plan to bad timing," not "what will happen."

1. Overview & Purpose

P-ZERO is a deterministic, year-by-year retirement simulation engine for a married couple planning a phased early retirement. Starting from 2026, it projects the growth, drawdown, tax liability, and longevity of five asset pools — Traditional (pre-tax), Roth, Taxable Brokerage, HSA, and 529 — across a multi-decade horizon, through four life phases: full-time accumulation, a "covered" bridge when only one spouse has stepped down, semi-retirement (both on consulting income), and full retirement.

"Deterministic" means one fixed path: every year uses the same nominal return (default 6%) and the same inflation rate (default 3%). This makes each year's math fully traceable — you can pick any row and reconcile every dollar — which is the tool's core strength. The deterministic core does not model market volatility; a good year and a crash year look identical. To test that risk directly, the Monte Carlo tool (top nav) re-runs the whole plan hundreds of times over random return sequences — see Section 18.

The engine runs each year through a consistent pipeline: determine life phase from each spouse's age → apply contributions (accumulation only) → compute forced RMDs → compute Roth conversions (bracket-filling) → pay tuition from 529 → pay healthcare from HSA → iteratively solve the tax-and-withdrawal loop (Section 8) → apply investment growth per your chosen timing convention (Section 17) → record the row.

Beyond the projection, four tools (top nav) help you act on it: Compare pins two plans side by side; Solve For inverts the model to find max spend, earliest retirement, or required portfolio; Monte Carlo stress-tests against random return sequences; and the seven KPI cards surface horizon, end wealth, after-tax legacy to heirs, lifetime tax, ACA subsidy, and 529 status. All are documented below.

The simulation runs entirely in your browser. No data is sent to any server; all calculations happen locally in JavaScript.

Important — for illustration and educational purposes only. P-ZERO is a personal planning sandbox. It is not financial, tax, investment, or legal advice, and nothing here is a recommendation to take (or not take) any action. Every projection rests on simplified assumptions — a single fixed return, approximated tax tables, hand-entered historical data, and many modeling shortcuts documented in Sections 19 and 20 — so real-world outcomes will differ, potentially by a lot. Do not make financial decisions based on this tool. Consult a qualified financial advisor, CPA, or attorney about your individual circumstances before acting.

2. Dollar Display Modes — and Why Ending ≠ Next Year's Starting

The engine always runs its internal math in nominal (future) dollars — this is required for correct tax-bracket, RMD, and Social Security calculations, since those are all defined in nominal-dollar terms. The Today's $ / Future $ toggle only changes how results are displayed:

  • Future $ (nominal): the raw dollar amounts that will actually move through the accounts in that calendar year.
  • Today's $ (inflation-adjusted, default): every figure in a given row is divided by that row's cumulative inflation factor (1.03n for year n at 3% inflation), converting it to today's purchasing power.

Important: in Today's $ mode, a year's Ending balance will NOT equal the next year's Starting balance. This is correct behavior, not a bug.

Internally the balances flow with perfect continuity: the nominal Ending balance of year N is exactly the nominal Starting balance of year N+1 (you can verify this by switching to Future $ mode, where the numbers match to the penny). But in Today's $ mode, year N's row is deflated by year N's inflation factor, while year N+1's row is deflated by year N+1's (larger) factor. The same nominal balance is therefore shown about 3% smaller in the next row — because a year of inflation has genuinely eroded its purchasing power.

Worked example (default inputs): 2026 ends with a nominal spendable balance of $3,030,623. In Future $ mode, 2027's Starting shows exactly $3,030,623. In Today's $ mode, 2027's Starting shows $2,942,352 — which is $3,030,623 ÷ 1.03. Nothing was lost; that money simply buys 3% less than it would have a year earlier.

This is an unavoidable property of real-dollar display: you can have within-row reconciliation (Starting + Contribs + Growth − outflows = Ending, all at one year's price level) or across-row continuity, but not both. This tool preserves within-row reconciliation, which is what makes each year's math auditable.

Sidebar inputs (balances, contributions, spending targets, healthcare costs, gift amounts, tuition costs) are always entered in today's dollars and are inflated internally to the nominal amount for the year they apply. The one exception is starting balances, which are 2026 dollars by definition (today = 2026 in this model).

3. Input Parameters Explained

Household Core

  • Birth month/year (H & W): Defaults: Husband Oct 1979, Wife Aug 1982. Ages are computed per calendar year (age = year − birth year) and drive every age-based trigger: retirement phases, catch-up eligibility, penalty thresholds, Medicare at 65, RMD age, and Social Security claiming.
  • Target EOL (default 95): The simulation runs until both spouses exceed this age. It also defines the "Portfolio Horizon" success test and the year used for the Net Worth at EOL KPI.

Age Milestones

  • Semi-retirement ages (H 55 / W 52 → both step down in 2034): Household drawdown begins only when the last full-time worker steps down — if one spouse steps down first, the gap years are the "Covered" phase (Section 7, Phase 1.5) with contributions at the configurable covered-% (default 50%). Once both have stepped down, all contributions stop, per-spouse 1099 consulting income begins (H $25K / W $100K by default; each spouse’s income runs from their own semi age to their own full age), healthcare costs begin, and portfolio withdrawals start covering any shortfall.
  • Full-retirement ages (H 57 / W 60 → 2042): Full retirement begins only when both spouses have reached their full age. 1099 income (and its SE tax) ends.
  • RMD age (default 75): When the older spouse reaches this age, forced minimum distributions from the Traditional bucket begin (Section 11).

Annual Contributions (accumulation years only)

  • Defaults: Traditional $70,000 • Roth $70,000 • Brokerage $70,000 • HSA $8,750 • 529 $24,000 — all entered in today's dollars and inflated each year. These are household combined figures (both spouses), not per-person IRS limits; adjust them to match your actual savings plan.
  • Catch-up toggle: when on, adds $8,000/yr to Traditional+Roth once either spouse is 50+, and $1,000/yr to HSA once either spouse is 55+ (inflation-scaled).
  • Brokerage contributions increase the brokerage cost basis dollar-for-dollar.

Asset Pool Balances & Brokerage Basis

  • Defaults: Traditional $1,000,000 • Roth $500,000 • Brokerage $1,000,000 • HSA $130,000 • 529 $260,000 (2026 starting values).
  • Brokerage Basis % (default 60%): the share of the brokerage balance that is original after-tax principal. Only the remaining gain portion of each brokerage withdrawal is taxed (at LTCG rates). The basis ratio is tracked and updated every year as contributions add basis and withdrawals consume it proportionally.
  • Brokerage Dividend Yield (default 2%) & % Qualified (default 85%): the annual dividend the taxable account throws off, and what share of it is "qualified" — the long-held-stock kind taxed at the lower long-term capital-gains rates, versus ordinary dividends (REITs, bond/money-market funds) taxed at regular income rates. See Section 12.

Distribution Strategy

  • Annual Living Target — Excl. Healthcare (default $149,000, today's $): the net, after-tax amount you want available for non-healthcare living expenses each retirement year. Taxes do NOT come out of this number — the engine grosses up the portfolio withdrawal so that taxes are paid on top and the full target lands in your pocket (Section 8). Healthcare, gifting, and tuition are also tracked separately on top.
  • Distribution model: fixed dollar target (above) or a percentage of the portfolio.
  • Withdrawal Order: Sequential (Brokerage → Traditional → Roth) or Pro-Rata. See Section 9.
  • Healthcare (Pre-65) $36,000 / Post-65 Medicare $18,000: combined household annual costs, today's $. See Section 14 for the split-age transition, the ACA subsidy inputs (toggle, $28K benchmark premium, household size, preserve-subsidy cap) and the IRMAA toggle.
  • Growth Timing: when contributions/withdrawals earn growth within a year. Default "Realistically Conservative" (half-year convention). See Section 17.

Roth Conversion, Social Security, Children

  • Roth conversion controls (toggle — off by default; start — "Auto" (shown as the younger spouse's age and start year, e.g. "53 (2035)"), or any manual age — bracket-fill cap or fixed amount) — Section 10.
  • Social Security controls (toggle — off by default, per-spouse claim ages and amounts, COLA, taxable %) — Section 13.
  • Children profiles (birth years, undergrad/grad costs and durations) and the gifting schedule — Section 15.
  • Survivor Scenario (toggle, first-to-pass, age at passing, survivor spending %) — documented as Phase 4 in Section 7.

Macro Inputs & Tax Tables

  • Nominal ROI (default 6%): applied identically to all five buckets every year.
  • Inflation (default 3%): inflates spending targets, healthcare, contributions, tax brackets, the standard deduction, and gift/tuition amounts; also drives the Today's $ deflator.
  • Federal ordinary brackets, LTCG brackets, standard deduction ($32,200 MFJ), state tax (2.90%), county/local tax (1.5% — Boone Co.), and the IRS Uniform Lifetime RMD divisors, and the heir tax rates that drive the After-Tax Legacy KPI, are all editable in the Tax & Other Details tab and feed the engine directly. Bracket thresholds are inflation-indexed each simulated year.

4. KPI Cards Explained

KPIWhat it means & how it's computed
Portfolio HorizonThe headline "will the money last" test — 529 is excluded. Shows "95+ (Secure)" if the spendable portfolio (Traditional + Roth + Brokerage + HSA) covers every year's needs to Target EOL. Depletion is detected by funding shortfall, not by balances literally hitting zero: the failure year is the first year the portfolio cannot supply more than a trivial amount (>$100 nominal) of the required withdrawal. This matters because withdrawals are capped at available balances, so tiny residual balances can linger even after the plan has effectively failed. On failure, the card shows both spouses’ ages at the failure year beneath the headline (the survivor’s age alone in a Survivor Scenario), and the chart’s red “Depleted” marker carries the same year-and-age annotation. The Brokerage Exhausted card carries the same age sub-line at its crossover year.
Net Worth at EOL (excl. 529)The spendable portfolio balance (Traditional + Roth + Brokerage + HSA) in the final simulated year, in the currently selected dollar mode. 529 is deliberately excluded so leftover education money can never mask a depleted retirement portfolio. Turns red whenever Portfolio Horizon has failed.
After-Tax Legacy (to heirs)The final estate's worth to non-spouse heirs, applying a per-bucket tax haircut the plain End Worth number hides: Roth × 100% (inherited tax-free), Brokerage × 100% (heirs get a step-up in basis that erases the gain), HSA and Traditional taxed at the editable heir tax rates (default 28% each) — because under the SECURE Act a non-spouse heir must drain an inherited Traditional within 10 years, paying ordinary tax on every dollar. 529 is excluded, like the other wealth KPIs. When this number sits well below End Worth, the gap is the deferred tax bomb your heirs would inherit; when they're nearly equal (as in the default plan, whose sequencing leaves an almost all-Roth estate), the estate is already tax-efficient. This is the missing half of the Roth-conversion decision: a conversion that looks unattractive on lifetime tax alone can pay off once its shrinking of the heirs' Traditional bomb is counted.

How to read it: compare this card directly against Net Worth at EOL sitting beside it. If the two match, you're doing great — your estate is essentially all Roth/brokerage, so nothing is lost to heir taxes and there's no bomb to defuse (this is the default plan's situation, thanks to the Brokerage→Traditional→Roth withdrawal order). If Legacy sits below End Worth, the gap is exactly the tax your heirs would owe on the leftover Traditional/HSA — a signal that Roth conversions (or spending Traditional sooner) could shrink it. To test the payoff: note the baseline, Pin as A, turn conversions on, and watch the Lifetime Tax delta (the cost to you) against the After-Tax Legacy delta (the gain to heirs) side by side — that comparison is the whole decision.
Brokerage ExhaustedThe first retirement year in which the brokerage account was fully drained by the year's withdrawal and the draw still needed more — i.e., the true crossover point where spending spills into Traditional/Roth. Under the default sequential order this is a normal, expected milestone, so it only displays as a warning if it coincides with overall portfolio failure.
Projected Lifetime TaxThe sum of all taxes (federal ordinary, LTCG, SE, state, county, penalties) across every simulated year. Each year's tax is converted to the selected dollar mode before summing — nominal dollars from different decades can't be meaningfully added, so in Today's $ mode each year's tax is deflated at its own factor first.
Lifetime ACA SubsidyThe sum of all ACA premium subsidies captured across the plan (each year converted to the selected dollar mode before summing, like Lifetime Tax). Hidden entirely while the ACA toggle is off (simple model); green when subsidies are being captured. Useful as the single number to watch when comparing conversion strategies against the subsidy cliff.
529 Status"Fully Funded" if the 529 covered every scheduled tuition year; "Underfunded" (red) if tuition ever exceeded the 529 balance, forcing the shortfall to be paid from Brokerage.

5. Combined Table Columns (Household Summary)

The Combined section shows spendable wealth only — Traditional + Roth + Brokerage + HSA. The 529 is excluded from Starting/Contribs/Growth/Ending here (it has its own detail section), because that money is earmarked for education and cannot fund living expenses without tax consequences. The columns are designed so that cash needs, cash sources, taxable income, and actual portfolio flows are cleanly separated (the MAGI column appears only when ACA or IRMAA is enabled, since it is what those programs read):

ColumnDefinition
StartingCombined spendable balance at the start of the year (= prior year's nominal Ending; see Section 2 for why the displayed Today's-$ values differ across rows).
ContribsTotal contributions to Trad + Roth + Brokerage + HSA. Non-zero only during full-time accumulation years; drops to $0 the year either spouse semi-retires.
GrowthInvestment growth on the four spendable buckets, per the selected growth timing convention (Section 17). Under the default half-year convention, the growth base is Starting + ½Contributions − ½Withdrawals.
Planned DistYour annual living target (default $149K, today's $, inflated). Pure lifestyle spending — excludes healthcare, gifting, Roth conversions, and taxes. This is a need, not a flow.
HealthcareTotal household healthcare cost for the year (pre-65 / split / Medicare tiers — Section 14). The HSA pays this first, tax-free; only the portion the HSA can't cover flows into the portfolio withdrawal.
IncomeGross 1099 consulting income (defaults H $25K / W $100K, today's $, inflated). Each spouse's income runs on their own window — from their semi-retirement age until their own full-retirement age — so this column steps down as each spouse individually stops consulting. Shown gross — SE tax is not netted out here; it appears in the Taxes column instead, so it is counted exactly once.
Roth ConvAmount converted Traditional → Roth this year (Section 10). Fully taxable as ordinary income, but it does not leave the portfolio — so it appears here and in Total Taxable, but never in Portfolio W/D.
Soc SecCombined gross Social Security benefits received (per-spouse claim ages; COLA-indexed). $0 while the SS toggle is off (the default).
Life EventOne-off outflows from the gifting schedule (entered in today's $, inflated to the gift year so the real value you intended is what's actually gifted).
Total TaxableThe gross ordinary-income pool feeding the tax engine, before the standard deduction: (1099 income − ½ SE-tax deduction) + RMDs + elective Traditional draws + Roth conversion + taxable share of SS. Note the ½ SE deduction is half of the SE tax (≈$7K on $100K of income), not half of the income. LTCG from brokerage gains is taxed separately on top and is not in this column.
MAGIModified Adjusted Gross Income for benefit-eligibility purposes: gross ordinary income + realized capital gains + full Social Security (taxed and untaxed portions). No deductions reduce it — which is why it differs from Total Taxable. Drives the ACA 400% FPL subsidy cliff in pre-65 years and the IRMAA tiers post-65 (via the two-year lookback). The column hides automatically when both the ACA and IRMAA toggles are off. Note how the same spending produces wildly different MAGI depending on the funding source: Traditional draws count fully, brokerage draws count only their gain portion, Roth draws count zero.
TaxesTotal tax drag for the year: federal ordinary + LTCG (federal and state/local on the gain) + SE tax + state + county + any 10% early-withdrawal penalty. Produced by the iterative solver (Section 8).
Portfolio W/DThe net cash that actually left the portfolio: Planned Dist + healthcare not covered by HSA + gifts + taxes − gross income − Social Security (floored at zero). Reconciles exactly to the sum of the per-bucket Dists columns (Trad + Roth + Brokerage elective draws + RMD used for spending). Roth conversions are excluded.
EndingSpendable balance at year end = Starting + Contribs + Growth − Portfolio W/D − HSA healthcare draw (the HSA is inside the combined total, so its healthcare payments reduce Ending even though they never touch Portfolio W/D). Any 529 tuition shortfall paid by Brokerage also reduces it.

Worked reconciliation (2034 — first drawdown year, default inputs, today's $):

Portfolio W/D = $149,000 (Planned) + $0 (healthcare — HSA covered all $36,000) + $0 (gifts) + $35,752 (taxes) − $125,000 (income, both 1099s active) − $0 (SS) = $59,751 — matching the 2034 row exactly. Ending = Starting + Growth − Portfolio W/D − $36,000 (HSA draw) − tuition paid by 529 (reflected only in the full-net-worth view). Every row reconciles this way at that row's price level.

6. Per-Account Detail Columns

Each bucket (Traditional, Roth, Brokerage, HSA & 529) has a hidden-by-default detail group — reveal them with the "Show Detail" buttons above the table. Columns follow a consistent pattern: Starting / Contribs / Growth / Dists / Ending, plus bucket-specific columns:

  • Traditional: Dists include forced RMDs plus elective draws; a Roth Conv column shows the amount leaving Traditional via conversion (reduces Traditional, increases Roth, never counts as a distribution to you).
  • Roth: Conversions arrive as inflows; elective Roth draws are tax-free (but incur the 10% penalty if taken before 59½ in this model).
  • Brokerage: withdrawals split proportionally into basis (tax-free return of principal) and gain (LTCG-taxed) using the tracked basis ratio. Excess RMDs beyond spending needs are reinvested here and add to basis.
  • HSA: Dists are healthcare payments (tax-free), made before any portfolio money is touched. The HSA is never tapped for non-medical spending in this model.
  • 529: Dists are tuition payments per the Children schedule (Section 15). Not part of the Combined columns.

Cross-check anytime: Trad Dists (elective + RMD used for spending) + Roth Dists + Brokerage Dists = Combined Portfolio W/D.

7. Life Phase Logic

Phase 1 — Accumulation (2026 → 2033 with defaults)

Both spouses working full-time. All five contribution streams flow (inflation-indexed, plus catch-ups when eligible). No withdrawals, no healthcare cost modeled (employer coverage assumed), no 1099 income.

Phase 1.5 — Covered (only when semi ages differ; none with current defaults)

If one spouse steps down while the other still works full-time, the household enters a "Covered" bridge phase: the working spouse's W2 income (which the model does not simulate) is assumed to fund all living costs, and employer coverage handles healthcare — so there are no portfolio draws, no healthcare cost, and no living-target withdrawal. Contributions continue at the configurable "Contribution % While One Spouse Still Works" (default 50%, reflecting one earner's remaining savings capacity). The semi-retired spouse's 1099 income is ignored (assumed absorbed into household coverage), and Roth conversions are suppressed — bracket-fill sizing would be blind to the unmodeled W2 income occupying the same brackets. The one exception: scheduled gifts remain portfolio-funded — a one-off six-figure transfer isn't plausibly cash-flowed from a single salary, so the conservative treatment draws it (plus its capital-gains gross-up) from the portfolio. True drawdown begins only when the LAST full-time worker steps down.

Phase 2 — Semi-Retirement (2034 → 2041 with defaults)

Begins the year both spouses have left full-time work — each past their own semi age (with defaults, both step down in 2034, so no Covered phase occurs). Contributions stop entirely. Per-spouse 1099 consulting income begins (H $25K / W $100K by default, today's $), subject to SE tax; each spouse's income runs from their own semi age to their own full age, so household income is $125K in 2034–2035, then $100K (W alone) from 2036 until her full retirement at 60 in 2042. Healthcare costs begin (pre-65 private rate). The living target, healthcare not covered by HSA, gifts, and all taxes are funded first by income, then by portfolio withdrawals. Roth conversions start per the Auto rule — the year after both spouses have stepped down (2035 with defaults) — sized to the bracket room left after 1099 income and any Traditional spending draws; a manual start age can override Auto, but conversions are always suppressed while any spouse still works full-time.

Phase 3 — Full Retirement (2042+ with defaults)

Begins only when both spouses have reached their full-retirement ages (both 57 → 2039). By this point each spouse's 1099 income has already ended at their own full-retirement age, so SE tax is gone. The portfolio (plus Social Security, if enabled) funds everything. Medicare transition occurs per-spouse at 65; RMDs begin at the RMD age; conversions continue while bracket room and Traditional balance remain.

Phase 4 — Survivor Scenario (optional, off by default)

When enabled in the sidebar, you choose which spouse passes first and at what age. The year of death itself still files MFJ (matching IRS rules) and is marked with a † in the ledger; from the following year onward the model captures the “widow’s penalty”: filing status flips to Single, so every ordinary and LTCG bracket threshold and the standard deduction are halved (exact for brackets through 24%; a close approximation above) — meaning roughly the same RMD and conversion income now faces twice the bracket pressure. Simultaneously: the survivor keeps the larger of the two Social Security benefits (from their own claim age) and the smaller one stops; the living target is scaled by the Survivor Spending % (default 75%); healthcare drops to one person (half the age-appropriate tier); only the survivor's own 1099 income continues (if still within their consulting window); RMD age, penalty age, and retirement-phase logic re-key to the survivor’s own age; the simulation runs to the survivor’s Target EOL; and a one-time step-up in basis is applied — the decedent’s half of the jointly-held brokerage’s unrealized gain becomes basis, materially reducing future LTCG and partially offsetting the bracket compression. This is a deterministic what-if, not a mortality forecast: use it to test whether your Roth conversion strategy leaves the survivor exposed to compressed Single brackets.

8. The Iterative Tax & Withdrawal Solver

Determining the year's withdrawal creates a circular problem: the withdrawal must cover taxes, but the taxes depend on which accounts the withdrawal comes from — Traditional draws add ordinary income (potentially crossing brackets), brokerage draws create capital gains, and pre-59½ draws add penalties. More withdrawal → more tax → more withdrawal.

The engine resolves this with an iterative solve, run fresh for every simulated year:

  1. Compute the base need: Planned Dist + healthcare not covered by HSA + gifts, minus cash sources (gross 1099 income + Social Security). This seeds the withdrawal estimate with no taxes yet.
  2. Simulate the account draws for that withdrawal per the chosen sequence, capped at available balances.
  3. Size the bracket-fill Roth conversion for this iteration (spending-aware): fill only the room remaining between (existing ordinary income + those elective Traditional spending draws) and the chosen bracket cap, further capped at the Traditional balance left after spending draws. Fixed-amount conversions are sized up-front instead and skip this step.
  4. Compute the full tax stack for that draw mix: federal ordinary tax on (income − ½SE deduction + RMD + elective Traditional draws + Roth conversion + taxable SS − standard deduction), LTCG on the brokerage gain portion (stacked on top of ordinary income against the LTCG brackets), state + county, SE tax, and the 10% early-withdrawal penalty on Trad/Roth draws if the older spouse is under 59½.
  5. Compute the ACA subsidy from this iteration's MAGI (when enabled), net it against the marketplace premium, apply the HSA to the remaining healthcare, and fold the uncovered healthcare into the need — the subsidy is discontinuous at the 400% FPL cliff, so an oscillation guard settles on the conservative no-subsidy branch if the solve ping-pongs across it.
  6. Set the new withdrawal = fixed needs + uncovered healthcare + that tax bill − cash sources; repeat from step 2 until the withdrawal changes by less than $1 (typically 3–6 iterations, hard cap 14).

Consequence — the living target is truly net: your $149K is what you keep. Taxes are grossed up on top of it and drawn additionally from the portfolio. In heavy Roth-conversion or Traditional-heavy years the total drain can be substantially larger than the target itself.

If the portfolio cannot supply the converged withdrawal (all accounts exhausted), the unmet portion is recorded as a funding shortfall — this is what triggers the Portfolio Horizon failure state (Section 4), even though small residual balances may technically remain.

9. Withdrawal Sequencing

Sequential (default): Brokerage → Traditional → Roth. Spend the taxable account first (only the gain portion is taxed, at favorable LTCG rates, and draining it early leaves low-taxable-income years for cheap Roth conversions), tap Traditional second (ordinary income, but often at low brackets by then), preserve Roth for last (tax-free growth compounds longest, best asset to inherit).

Pro-Rata: each year's withdrawal is split across Traditional, Roth, and Brokerage in proportion to their current balances. Smooths the tax character of withdrawals over time rather than concentrating account types in phases.

The solver (Section 8) prices both strategies correctly — Traditional portions generate ordinary tax, brokerage portions generate LTCG on the gain share, Roth portions are tax-free — and the resulting tax difference feeds back into the withdrawal size. The HSA sits outside this sequence entirely (healthcare only), as does the 529 (tuition only).

10. Roth Conversion Ladder

The conversion ladder is off by default (the RMD tax-torpedo and its IRMAA knock-on are a genuine argument for conversions, but whether they pay off is scenario-specific — so the conservative baseline leaves them off and lets you switch them on to test). When enabled (with a start age), each year the engine can move money from Traditional to Roth, paying ordinary income tax now in exchange for tax-free growth forever after and a smaller future RMD base. Two modes:

  • Fill bracket to X% (default) — spending-aware: the conversion is sized inside the iterative tax solver, after that year’s elective Traditional spending draws are known. It fills only the room remaining between (existing ordinary income + Traditional spending draws) and the top of your chosen bracket, so combined ordinary income lands at the cap and never over it. Spending has priority over conversion for both bracket room and Traditional balance — in years where spending draws alone exceed the cap, the conversion is $0, exactly as a human advisor would size it. When "Cap Roth conversions to preserve subsidy" is on (the default) and a marketplace subsidy is live, the conversion is additionally capped so MAGI lands at the 400% FPL cliff rather than over it — unless spending alone already blew the cliff, in which case the subsidy is lost anyway and the bracket cap governs alone. Capped at the remaining Traditional balance; brackets are the actual editable, inflation-indexed table.
  • Fixed amount: converts a set dollar amount per year regardless of bracket position.

Start timing: the default is Auto — the year after the later-retiring spouse steps down (2035 with defaults), avoiding bracket-filling on top of any unmodeled partial-year W2 income in the transition year; any manual age from the same dropdown overrides it. Either way, conversions are suppressed during accumulation and Covered years, since a still-working spouse's W2 income would invisibly occupy the same brackets. Worked example (defaults): in 2035, $125K of 1099 income occupies most of the lower brackets and the engine converts $127,431 to reach the 22% cap; in 2036, after H fully retires and income drops to $100K, the conversion grows to $150,665. The conversion appears in Roth Conv and Total Taxable, its tax lands in Taxes (and is grossed into the withdrawal), and the money itself moves Traditional → Roth without ever appearing in Portfolio W/D.

Because the conversion and the withdrawal/tax solve are computed together, the conversion’s tax is grossed into the year’s withdrawal and its LTCG-stacking effect on brokerage gains is fully priced. Fixed-amount mode remains deliberately naive (converts the set amount regardless of bracket position or spending draws). The 5-year seasoning rule on converted funds is not enforced (see Limitations).

11. Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)

Starting the year the older spouse reaches the RMD age (default 75), the engine forces a distribution of Traditional balance ÷ the IRS Uniform Lifetime divisor for that age (table editable in Tax & Other Details). RMDs are always fully taxable ordinary income and are included in Total Taxable.

RMD cash is used to fund the year's spending need first; any excess beyond what spending required is reinvested into the Brokerage account (adding to cost basis) — it never disappears. Simplification: the model holds one shared Traditional bucket keyed to the older spouse's age; real RMDs are per-person, per-account, so households with large age gaps or unequal Traditional balances will see timing differences vs. reality.

12. Tax Calculations

Ordinary Income Tax

Applied to: 1099 income (less the ½ SE-tax deduction), RMDs, elective Traditional withdrawals, Roth conversions, and the taxable share of Social Security. The standard deduction ($32,200 MFJ, inflation-indexed) is subtracted, then the editable MFJ marginal brackets (thresholds inflation-indexed) apply.

Long-Term Capital Gains

Only the gain portion of brokerage withdrawals (per the tracked basis ratio) is LTCG income. It stacks on top of ordinary taxable income against the editable LTCG brackets (0%/15%/20% structure), so low-ordinary-income years can realize gains at 0%. State + county rates also apply to the gain.

Self-Employment Tax

15.3% on 92.35% of gross 1099 income during semi-retirement, summed across both spouses’ active incomes (≈$14K on $100K combined; the Social Security wage-base cap is not modeled). Half of the SE tax (≈$7K — not half the income) is deducted from ordinary taxable income. Income is displayed gross and SE tax sits in the Taxes column, so it is counted exactly once in the cash-flow math.

State & Local (Indiana / Boone County)

Flat state rate (default 2.90% — Indiana’s locked-in 2027+ rate; the 2026 rate is 2.95% and conditional trigger cuts could go lower after 2030) + county LIT (default 1.5% for Boone County — verify your exact rate on Indiana DOR Departmental Notice #1, as county rates can adjust each January and October). Both apply to ordinary income and to realized capital gains — Indiana taxes long-term gains as ordinary income with no preferential rate, and fully taxes IRA/401(k) withdrawals and Roth conversions. Social Security is exempt from Indiana state and county tax, and the “State Exempts Social Security” checkbox (default on) removes the taxable-SS portion from the state/local base accordingly. Deliberately not modeled (immaterial at this income level): Indiana’s small personal exemptions ($1,000/person, extra at 65+) and the pension deduction (which does not apply to IRA distributions).

Early Withdrawal Penalty

10% federal penalty on elective Traditional and Roth draws while the older spouse is under 59½. Simplifications: keyed to the older spouse's age; applied to full Roth draws (real Roth contribution basis can come out penalty-free); no 72(t)/SEPP, Rule of 55, or other exceptions.

Brokerage Dividend Drag

A taxable brokerage account pays out dividends each year — cash you owe tax on whether or not you sell anything, unlike the tax-sheltered Traditional/Roth/HSA buckets. That unavoidable annual tax is the "drag." The engine estimates it as Dividend Yield × brokerage balance (default 2%), split into a qualified slice and an ordinary slice. Qualified dividends (default 85% — the kind from U.S. and many foreign stocks held long enough) are taxed at the preferential long-term capital-gains rates (0/15/20%), stacked on top of ordinary income just like realized gains; the ordinary slice (REITs, money-market and bond funds, or stocks not held long enough) is taxed at regular income rates. A portfolio of broad U.S. index funds runs high (~85–100% qualified); heavy REIT/bond holdings pull it lower. Both are added to MAGI, so dividends can nudge you toward the ACA cliff or an IRMAA tier. The dividends themselves stay invested (they're part of the 6% return, not an extra inflow); because they are after-tax principal, reinvesting them adds to cost basis — which lowers the taxable gain on future sales. Net effect: a small tax paid every year, partly offset later by reduced capital-gains tax when those shares are sold. Set the yield to 0 to switch the whole feature off.

Not modeled

AMT, NIIT (3.8%), IRMAA surcharges, ACA premium subsidies/cliffs, state-specific 529 recapture, qualified dividends on un-sold holdings, tax-loss harvesting, and filing-status changes (e.g., survivor filing single).

13. Social Security

Off by default — the default projection deliberately excludes SS as a conservatism margin; toggle it on to include it. Each spouse has an independent claim age (defaults H:70, W:67) and an annual benefit stated in today's dollars ($25,000 each — intentionally conservative haircuts vs. statement estimates).

Benefits are indexed by the separate SS COLA input (default 3%) from 2026 — a simplification of how SSA actually indexes to claim age and applies COLA after. The editable "taxable %" (default 85%) of benefits joins ordinary taxable income; the true IRS provisional-income phase-in formula is not modeled (at this household's income level, 85% is almost always the real answer anyway). Benefits reduce the required portfolio withdrawal dollar-for-dollar in the solver. Indiana does not tax SS, so state/county tax is not applied to benefits (per the State Exempts SS checkbox). Spousal top-ups are not modeled; survivor benefits are handled only via the optional Survivor Scenario (Section 7).

14. Healthcare: MAGI, ACA Subsidies & IRMAA

The one-sentence mental model:

One yardstick — MAGI — is read by two programs: before 65, LOW MAGI earns you subsidies (ACA marketplace); after 65, HIGH MAGI earns you penalties (Medicare IRMAA). Everything below is detail on that sentence.

14.1 — The base costs (what you budget)

The two sidebar inputs are all-in gross household costs in today's dollars, inflation-indexed: Pre-65 ($36,000 default) = unsubsidized marketplace premiums + deductibles + copays for the couple; Post-65 Medicare ($18,000 default) = Part B/D + Medigap + out-of-pocket. Costs begin at semi-retirement and follow the household's age mix: both under 65 → full pre-65 rate; one on each side of 65 → half of each; both 65+ → full Medicare rate. Everything in this section only ever adjusts these bases: ACA can reduce the pre-65 side; IRMAA can increase the post-65 side; neither ever touches the other side of the 65 line.

Want the simple model? Turn both toggles off → the engine runs flat $36,000 / $18,000 (inflation-indexed, split-age blended) with zero ACA/IRMAA adjustments — verified identical to the pre-ACA engine, cell for cell. The MAGI column and the Lifetime ACA Subsidy card hide automatically so the interface matches the simpler model.

14.2 — MAGI: the yardstick everything reads

MAGI (Modified Adjusted Gross Income) is the government's measure of "how much income did you really have this year," used for benefit eligibility. Crucially, no deductions reduce it — which is why it differs from the Total Taxable column.

The most important example in this guide: identical spending, three wildly different MAGIs

Same lifestyle, a quarter-million dollars of MAGI difference. This is why your account order and Roth-conversion strategy — not any toggle — control your healthcare costs.

14.3 — ACA subsidies before 65: the math, worked

Under post-2025 law, subsidies exist only while MAGI ≤ 400% of the Federal Poverty Level (FPL is editable in Tax & Other Details; household of 2 → cliff at $84,600 today's $). Within eligibility, you contribute a sliding 2.1%–9.96% of MAGI toward the benchmark premium (the $28K SLCSP input — the subsidizable slice of the $36K; the ~$8K OOP remainder is never subsidized); the subsidy pays the rest.

Worked example — MAGI $60,000, household of 2:

ratio = 60,000 / 21,150 FPL = 2.84× → applicable % ≈ 9.46%
your contribution = 9.46% × 60,000 = $5,678
subsidy = $28,000 benchmark − $5,678 = $22,322
net healthcare = $36,000 − $22,322 = $13,678 (vs $36,000 unsubsidized)

14.4 — Reading the Healthcare cell sub-lines

The big number in the cell is always the TOTAL you spend that year. The small sub-line is the explanation of how it got there — never an addition on top:

$9,007 −$26,993 ACA  → you spend $9,007; the label explains: $36,000 sticker minus a $26,993 subsidy
$24,355 +$6,355 IRMAA → you spend $24,355; the label explains: $18,000 base plus a $6,355 surcharge

A $36,000 + $6,355 = $42,355 year is impossible: ACA only touches the pre-65 base (downward), IRMAA only touches the post-65 base (upward). In a split year one cell can carry both sub-lines — each acting on its own half.

14.5 — IRMAA after 65: the penalty, worked

Medicare charges each enrolled person a premium surcharge when MAGI from two years earlier crossed a tier — so an age-63 decision sets an age-65 bill.

It's not a rolling window or a multi-year penalty. It's a one-to-one mapping with a 2-year delay. Each year's Medicare premium is determined by the MAGI from exactly one tax year — two years prior (the most recent return the SSA has on file). So:

  • One high-MAGI year → exactly one penalized year, arriving two years later. Not "penalized for the next 2 years."
  • Each year resets independently. There's no memory, no averaging, no persistence.
  • Sustained high MAGI does produce a sustained surcharge — but only because every high year generates its own penalized year downstream. The penalty stream is simply your MAGI stream, shifted two years and run through the tier table.
  • The danger zone therefore starts at age 63, not 65: age-63 MAGI sets the first Medicare premium. High MAGI at 62 or earlier maps to premium years before enrollment and never bites — the classic reason to finish big Roth conversions by 62.

14.7 — "The ACA toggle doesn't seem to do anything"

The toggle doesn't create subsidies — low MAGI does. The toggle only permits the model to count them. If every marketplace-eligible year has MAGI over the cliff (e.g., with conversions off, Traditional funds those years at ~$500K MAGI), the subsidy is $0 in every year and the toggle changes nothing — correctly. Diagnostic checklist: (1) Lifetime ACA Subsidy KPI reads $0? (2) Scan the MAGI column across pre-65 retirement years — is any year under ~$84,600? (3) If not, no toggle will help; only a strategy that changes the funding source of those years (conversions draining Traditional, Roth/basis-heavy withdrawals) can get you under the cliff.

Funding order (unchanged): the HSA pays healthcare first, tax-free, up to its balance (Medicare premiums and IRMAA are HSA-qualified); only the uncovered remainder enters the portfolio withdrawal and is grossed up for taxes. A subsidy therefore reduces real account outflows dollar-for-dollar — first preserving HSA, then shrinking Portfolio W/D. The living target still excludes healthcare entirely; these costs ride on top of the $149K. A 3-year subsidy extension has passed the House — the toggle exists precisely because this law may change.

15. Children: College Funding & Gifting

The Children section defines per-child education profiles that drive the 529's tuition schedule automatically — nothing is hardcoded. For each child: birth year, annual undergrad cost and years, and an optional grad-school block with its own cost and years. Undergrad starts the year the child turns 18; grad school starts immediately after undergrad ends. All costs are today's dollars, inflated to each tuition year.

Defaults: two children (born 2012 and 2015), each $30,500/yr × 4 undergrad years and $50,000/yr × 2 grad years — producing overlapping tuition from 2030 through 2038, including years where one child's grad school overlaps the other's undergrad.

Funding: tuition is paid from the 529 first. If the 529 runs dry, the shortfall is paid from Brokerage (reducing basis proportionally) and the 529 Status KPI flips to "Underfunded."

Gifting schedule: year/amount rows for one-off transfers (weddings, home down-payments, major gifts). Amounts are today's dollars — the engine inflates them to the gift year so the recipient gets the intended real value — and they appear in the Life Event column, funded through the normal withdrawal sequence.

16. 529 & HSA: Spendable vs Total Net Worth

The app draws a deliberate line between spendable retirement wealth (Traditional + Roth + Brokerage + HSA) and total net worth (those four + 529):

  • The Combined table columns, the Portfolio Horizon KPI, and the Net Worth at EOL KPI all use spendable wealth — a leftover 529 can never mask a depleted retirement portfolio.
  • The 529 remains fully visible in its own detail section, and the net-worth chart has independent toggles (HSA shown by default; 529 hidden by default); axis labels use compact $X.XXM formatting while the hover tooltip shows exact dollars per bucket.
  • The HSA counts as spendable because healthcare is a genuine, guaranteed living expense that the HSA directly funds tax-free — but the model only ever uses it for healthcare, never general spending.

If you ever raided the 529 for non-education spending (not modeled): contributions come out tax- and penalty-free; only the earnings portion is taxed as ordinary income plus a 10% federal penalty. A beneficiary can also roll up to $35,000 (lifetime) of a long-held 529 into their Roth IRA under current rules. State tax-credit recapture varies by state and isn't modeled.

Monte Carlo Simulation

Everything else in this tool is deterministic: every year earns exactly your ROI input, so "95+ Secure" really means "secure on the average path." Monte Carlo (top-nav button) instead runs your plan hundreds of times, each with a different random sequence of yearly returns and inflation, and reports how often it survives — exposing sequence-of-returns risk: a crash early in retirement is far more damaging than the same crash late, something a flat rate cannot see. It's the single most important lens for a long horizon that starts with big withdrawals. Only returns and inflation vary; every other input (spending, contributions, conversions, SS, ages, taxes) stays exactly as you set it, and your live plan is untouched — inputs are snapshotted and restored around the run.

Two methods, two questions:

  • Historical block bootstrap resamples real 1972–2025 return sequences in 5-year blocks — sampling each year's actual return and its matching inflation together, so both the crash-and-recovery momentum and the real return/inflation relationship stay intact. No recentering: it tests your plan against history's genuine pattern of good and bad timing. Because the sampled sequences carry history's actual average return (which was generous), nominal balances over a multi-decade horizon can compound to large numbers — the tool deflates ending values to today's dollars, but focus on the success rate and the fan's shape rather than the absolute peak. An optional stress mode extends the sampling pool back to 1928, adding the 1929 Great Depression and the 1966–1982 stagflation era — the worst sequences a retiree could face, which the modern window omits. It’s off by default (baseline = modern regime); turning it on is a deliberate tail-risk stress test and will lower the success rate.
  • Parametric normal draws each year independently from a bell curve centered on your ROI input with an editable volatility (default 12%, roughly a 70/30 portfolio). With no mean reversion and full volatility drag, it's deliberately more pessimistic. It answers "how robust is this around my assumption?"

Output (ephemeral — shown only after a run, closable): a success-rate headline (green ≥90%, amber ≥75%, red below), a fan chart of net worth over time (solid median with a shaded 10th–90th-percentile band), and the ending-net-worth percentiles. A rough rule of thumb is that 85–90%+ is a comfortable plan; lower means the plan works on average but is fragile to bad timing. Because MC honors your current toggles, running it with conversions on vs. off (or different spending) is a real robustness test, not just an average-case one.

Failure timing. When some runs fail, the results also report two depletion years: the typical failure (the median of all failed runs — the characteristic year a failing plan runs dry) and the earliest (the unluckiest run's depletion year). The earliest is the more volatile of the two — it shifts between runs and gets worse the more simulations you run, since more draws give bad luck more chances — so read it as "roughly how early could it go," not a fixed number.

Anatomy of a typical failure. If any runs failed, a small chart shows a representative failed run — the one whose depletion year sits at the median of all failures, so it reflects the characteristic failure rather than a freak extreme. It plots that run's year-by-year returns (losses in the first decade flagged in red) alongside its net-worth path sliding to depletion. Its purpose is to make sequence-of-returns risk visible: the failing runs almost always share the same signature — a market drop early in retirement, while withdrawals are largest relative to the balance, that sends the portfolio into a spiral it never recovers from. The specific run changes each time you press Run (it's a random draw), but the shape of the failure is the durable lesson.

How to read the results

  • Success rate is the bottom line — the share of runs where the money lasts to Target EOL, and the most stable figure (it barely moves between runs). Treat it as the "will I be OK?" number.
  • Percentiles are thresholds, not floors. "10th percentile = $X" means the worst 10% of runs ended at or below $X — not that $X is the worst case. Runs below it may have ended near zero or failed entirely years early (a plan bankrupt in 2050 shows ~$0 ending wealth but really failed far worse). The true downside lives below the 10th percentile, which is why the success rate is more trustworthy than any tail number.
  • The median is the middle, not the likely value. Half of runs end below it, half above; the outcomes are spread across the whole range, not clustered at the median. "50% chance of ending above the median" is correct; "high chance of ending near the median" is not.
  • Tail numbers wobble; that's sampling noise. Each run draws a fresh random set of sequences, so the 10th/90th percentiles jump around between runs. The median moves less, the success rate least. Raise the simulation count (1,000 decent, 5,000 stable) to steady the percentiles.

Methodology & data provenance

The historical method is a block bootstrap. The engine holds a hardcoded table of annual figures — S&P 500 total return, 10-year Treasury total return, and CPI inflation — for 1972–2025 (and 1928–1971 when stress mode is on). Each year those three are blended to a single portfolio return using a 70% stock / 30% bond mix, paired with that year's actual inflation. To build one simulated future, the engine picks a random 5-year block from history, then another, and another, until it has enough years to cover the horizon — sampling in blocks rather than single years so that real multi-year patterns (a crash followed by its recovery, a stagflation stretch) survive intact instead of being shuffled away. Returns and inflation always travel together as the same historical year's pair, preserving their real relationship. It runs the whole plan once per simulated future (hundreds of times), recording whether it survived and the year-by-year net worth, then reports the success rate and the 10/50/90 percentile bands.

The parametric method instead ignores history and draws each year's return independently from a normal (bell-curve) distribution centered on your ROI input, with an editable volatility. Because each year is independent, it has no mean reversion and suffers full volatility drag, making it deliberately more pessimistic than the historical method — a useful "what if the future is choppier than the past" counterweight.

Honest caveat on the data. The historical figures are hand-entered approximations of the public record, rounded and assembled for this tool — not a live feed from an authoritative source, and 2025 is partial/estimated. They are shape-accurate (the real crashes, recoveries, and inflation spikes are present in roughly the right magnitudes), which is what matters for stress-testing sequence risk, but they are not basis-point exact. Do not treat any specific percentile as precise. The data is frozen in the file and never updated at runtime; the app makes no network calls. This is a planning sandbox, not a market simulator — see the overall disclaimer.

Solve For (Max Spend / Earliest Retirement / Required Portfolio)

The Solve For button in the top nav inverts the model: instead of you setting an input and reading the outcome, it searches for the input value that hits a target outcome — running the full engine ~20 times behind the scenes (a binary search) to converge on the answer. Three targets:

  • Max sustainable annual spend — the highest Annual Living Target that still survives to Target EOL.
  • Earliest safe full-retirement year — the earliest year both spouses can fully retire with the plan still surviving; it sets both full-retirement ages to land on that year.
  • Required starting portfolio — scales your starting balances to the minimum that survives. You choose which buckets scale (all / spendable only / brokerage only), since "how much do I need" has different answers depending on which pot the money comes from.

Success means the portfolio never depletes before Target EOL and (if the cushion box is checked, default $250,000) ends at or above that cushion — uncheck it for the true bare-survival maximum. Crucially, every solve honors all your current toggles (SS, Roth conversions, ACA, IRMAA, survivor, dividends): "max spend" means max spend given everything else you've set, so flipping a toggle and re-solving shows how that lever moves the answer. The solved value is shown first; Apply to inputs writes it into the matching field (and offers Undo), or Cancel to leave your plan untouched. Probing never disturbs your inputs — they're snapshotted and restored around every search.

A/B Scenario Compare

Click Compare in the top nav to hold one plan side-by-side against another. Tune the app to your baseline first, then click Compare — it freezes the current plan as "A" and opens the Compare bar in one step. Keep editing — the live plan is "B". Each KPI card grows a second line showing A's value and the B−A delta (green = B is better: later depletion, higher end wealth, lower lifetime tax, more subsidy captured), and the net-worth chart overlays A's spendable trajectory as a dashed line behind B's stacked areas. Re-pin A refreshes the baseline to the current plan; Swap A↔B loads A's inputs back into the app (so you can keep iterating from the baseline) while the plan you were viewing becomes the new A; Clear ends the comparison. Both scenarios can be renamed. A is a frozen snapshot of computed results, not a live model, so it doesn't recompute when you change inputs — and it's session-only (a page reload clears it). One caution: if you pinned A in one dollar mode (Today's $ vs Future $) and switch B to the other, the End Worth delta flags a ⚠ since the two aren't directly comparable.

19. Key Assumptions & Simplifications

  • One fixed nominal return (6%) for every bucket, every year — no volatility, no glide path, no asset-allocation differences between account types.
  • One fixed inflation rate (3%) drives spending, healthcare, contributions, brackets, deductions, tuition, and gifts alike (SS has its own COLA input, defaulted equal).
  • Tax law is frozen: 2026 MFJ brackets/deduction inflate forever; no TCJA-style regime changes, no future legislation.
  • Single shared buckets per type (one Traditional, one Roth, etc.) rather than per-spouse accounts; RMDs and penalties key off the older spouse's age.
  • Brokerage basis is tracked as a single blended ratio, not tax lots; each withdrawal realizes gains proportionally.
  • Contribution inputs are taken as-entered (inflation-indexed) — IRS contribution limits are not enforced.
  • Healthcare costs are flat within each tier (no age-based escalation beyond inflation, no long-term-care shock modeling).
  • All flows are annual; intra-year timing exists only through the growth convention.
  • No Monte Carlo / sequence-of-returns analysis in this version — a single deterministic path only.

20. Known Limitations

  • Roth 5-year seasoning rules (for conversions and earnings) are not enforced — early Roth access is modeled as available with only the 10% penalty pre-59½.
  • Roth withdrawal ordering (contributions → conversions → earnings) is not modeled; penalties may be overstated for Roth draws.
  • The SS taxability phase-in, spousal/survivor benefits, and claiming-age benefit adjustments are simplified to flat inputs.
  • ACA subsidies (a major real-world interaction with Roth conversions pre-65) and IRMAA (post-65) are not modeled — conversion sizing here optimizes brackets only.
  • Fixed-amount Roth conversions are sized without regard to bracket position or same-year spending draws (by design); only bracket-fill mode is spending-aware. Single filing (survivor) brackets are approximated as half of MFJ.
  • Survivor Scenario simplifications: Single brackets are approximated as exactly half of MFJ (slightly off above the 24% bracket); the survivor SS benefit is simplified to the larger of the two configured amounts from the survivor’s own claim age; the qualifying-surviving-spouse filing status (2 years with dependents) is not modeled; only one death event is supported.
  • State/county taxes are flat rates with no deductions or credits; the only state-specific exclusion modeled is Indiana’s Social Security exemption. Scheduled/conditional Indiana rate cuts after 2027 are not modeled (the editable rate is fixed for the whole run).
  • Depletion detection uses a $100 nominal shortfall tolerance; sub-$100 annual gaps won't flag failure.
  • After-Tax Legacy simplifications: a single flat heir tax rate is applied to the whole inherited Traditional/HSA balance (real heirs drain it over up to 10 years across their own brackets); state estate/inheritance tax is not modeled (Indiana has none), the federal estate exemption is assumed not binding, and no growth during the heirs' 10-year window is modeled.
  • Dividend simplification: dividends are modeled as a flat yield on the brokerage balance with a fixed qualified/ordinary split; real dividends vary by holding, and return-of-capital distributions and dividend growth over time are not separately modeled.
  • Covered-phase assumption: the still-working spouse's W2 income is not simulated — the model assumes it fully funds living costs and healthcare during those years, with no surplus saved beyond the configured contribution %. If the salary couldn't actually cover the household budget, those years are modeled too favorably.
  • ACA simplifications: the applicable-percentage schedule is a fixed 2026 table (source-editable constant); MAGI below 138% FPL is floored rather than modeling Medicaid; premiums are not age-curved (one flat benchmark input); repayment/reconciliation of advance credits is not modeled; pending subsidy-extension legislation is handled only via the toggle.
  • IRMAA simplifications: tier thresholds are all inflation-indexed (in law the top tier is frozen until 2028); the lookback year's filing status is assumed to match the current year's; SSA-44 life-event appeals are not modeled.

21. Glossary

Nominal vs Real Dollars: Nominal = the literal dollar figures of a future year. Real (today's $) = nominal deflated by cumulative inflation to today's purchasing power. See Section 2.
Funding Shortfall: The portion of a year's required withdrawal the portfolio could not supply. The engine's true depletion signal.
Spendable Net Worth: Traditional + Roth + Brokerage + HSA — wealth available (directly or via healthcare) for retirement living. Excludes the 529.
Gross-Up: Withdrawing extra to cover the taxes generated by the withdrawal itself, so the target amount arrives net. Solved iteratively each year.
RMD: Required Minimum Distribution — IRS-mandated annual withdrawal from pre-tax accounts starting at the RMD age, computed from the Uniform Lifetime divisor table.
LTCG: Long-Term Capital Gains — the tax treatment of the gain portion of brokerage withdrawals; stacks on ordinary income against its own 0/15/20% brackets.
Cost Basis: The after-tax principal in the brokerage account. Withdrawals return basis tax-free and realize gains proportionally.
SE Tax: Self-Employment tax (15.3% on 92.35% of 1099 income). Half of the tax itself is deductible from ordinary income.
Roth Conversion Ladder: Systematically converting Traditional to Roth in low-bracket years to reduce lifetime taxes and future RMDs.
Sequence Risk: The danger of poor returns early in retirement permanently impairing a portfolio under withdrawals. Not captured by this deterministic model.
Widow’s Penalty: The tax squeeze on a surviving spouse: income stays similar but Single filing status halves bracket widths and the standard deduction. Modeled by the Survivor Scenario.
Step-Up in Basis: At death, the decedent’s share of appreciated taxable assets resets its cost basis to market value, erasing that share’s unrealized gain for tax purposes.
MFJ: Married Filing Jointly — the assumed federal filing status throughout.
IRMAA: Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount — Medicare premium surcharges per enrolled person, based on MAGI from two years prior. Modeled (toggleable) with 2026 tiers.
MAGI: Modified Adjusted Gross Income — the benefit-eligibility yardstick (no deductions reduce it). Drives the ACA cliff pre-65 and IRMAA tiers post-65. See Section 14.2.
SLCSP / Benchmark Premium: The second-lowest-cost Silver marketplace plan — the reference premium the ACA subsidy is computed against.
Subsidy Cliff: Under post-2025 law, ACA premium credits end abruptly at 400% of FPL — one extra dollar of MAGI forfeits the entire subsidy.
ACA: Affordable Care Act marketplace coverage for pre-65 retirees. Subsidies (and their interaction with Roth-conversion MAGI) are modeled — see Section 14.
COLA: Cost of Living Adjustment applied to Social Security benefits (separate input from general inflation).
